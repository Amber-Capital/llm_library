Is leveraged beta bad? Of course not! As long as you know what strategy your ETF fund manager, hedge fund manager, and subscription service provider are offering, and are comfortable with the risk you hold and most importantly it costs you virtually nothing to implement, leveraged beta is perfectly fine. If it is low cost to implement and matches your investment risk tolerance it is actually ideal.

But it’s not alpha, takes no skill whatsoever, and unfortunately when packaged by others on your behalf is usually expensive to buy. The sellers of leveraged beta strategy either are confused about what they are selling you and think they have something special that they add to the process (Alpha) or they are simply gaslighting you to charge high fees and make money for themselves.

You don’t need much help at all to implement a beta portfolio with the appropriate amount of leverage for your risk tolerance. But perhaps more importantly it is extremely unlikely that you could possibly assess what a portfolio manager, etf manager, or leveraged beta subscription service provider is actually doing and so following them because they have “outperformed” the SPX is likely a bad decision and probably an expensive one at a minimum.

Before I talk specifically about leveraged beta I have written two twitter threads related to this topic that are worth a look for background on this topic. This one says basically everyone is gaslighting you about their investment strategy and you are unlikely to be able tell what’s good.

This one discusses the word leverage. The great irony regarding leverage is the most insidious form of leverage uses no direct borrowing/margin of financial leverage at all! Leveraged Beta providers brag about their outperformance while at the same time gaslighting you with claims that they use no leverage at all. They speak literal truth as they are never more than 100% long. But direct leverage is only one source of “Leveraged Beta” This thread discuss leverage in all its forms. Its not a bad word but an investment without any direct leverage can be much much more leveraged than a portfolio that holds modest (and importantly cheap) financial leverage.

**What the heck is beta anyway?**

Beta is passive long only investing. It’s investing savings as you save in a portfolio of stocks and bonds and perhaps other assets like gold, BTC, commodities, real assets, and perhaps private market financial assets. The idea is simple. Holding risky things for the long term is better than saving in cash. I happen to have a beta approach that is diversified across all countries and all liquid public market assets. Is that the best form of beta? It’s fine. As an example DS Beta which is a simple weighting of $TLT, $TIP, $SPY, $GLD, and a commodity ETF has been about the same as SPY TLT 60/40. underperforming over the last 20 years on a raw return basis but also experiencing lower realized volatility and less bad drawdowns. Is it great? Meh. If leveraged to 60/40 risk target it would have outperformed. It has a better Sharpe and other risk management metrics but as my earlier embedded thread says who knows. More importantly who cares? Either portfolio is mostly fine. Its been a bull market for financial assets for decades even though 2007-2008 and 2020 are included in the sample size solid compounding has occurred. Beta is what every investor should hold. Best thing is its cheap to implement and you takes no skills whatsover. Just patience and a long horizon.

**Always own beta. But leveraged beta?**

Perhaps the most important part of investing is setting a risk target you can live with. Ill repeat a section of past work to explain why.

> ***Why target a risk level?***
> 
> The question should be, Why not? Currently, most money managers assess their clients’ risk preferences via a questionnaire. The idea is to establish a risk target. Once determined, investors’ expectations can be better managed about expected returns, ex ante portfolio volatility, and drawdown risk, so when things go badly investors hopefully can avoid emotionally exiting their long-term plan. For the person managing the money (whether a DIY personal investor or a professional portfolio manager) a risk target helps a manager size positions, seek real diversification, properly leverage (or hold cash), and be aware of potential drawdown risk. **We will not spend much time on the concept but strongly recommend that every investor understand themselves and how they may react in adverse portfolio situations.**
> 
> Here is our view on setting a risk target. There is a level of change in wealth that
> 
> 1\. Is absolute noise.
> 
> 2\. Is slightly annoying.
> 
> 3\. Pisses one off but does not cause upset or any real pain or stress.
> 
> 4\. Causes a loss of sleep.
> 
> 5\. Causes an action to ease the pain.
> 
> 6\. Causes a life changing loss.
> 
> 7\. Causes a loss so horrible that one cannot ever recover one’s current lifestyle.
> 
> These levels are personal to everyone. But we assure you everyone has levels like this. We have learned that 5-7 are not something we are willing to experience. We have experienced a level 5 event often and close to a level 6 and do not want to have that experience again. So, we operate somewhere in the range of 3-4. This range places us at a risk target we can manage, and we accept the expected return that is consistent with that level of risk. We do not want to be all-in. Staying in the game is much more important.
> 
> If you have never experienced a level 4 or 5, it is fair to say you are not taking enough risk but ideally you do not have to experience them and can just imagine them. The risk of experiencing a level 5 event is that it could turn into a level 6 event (which could be a life changing loss) or a level 7 event (which would be a loss so horrible you could not recover to anything like your current lifestyle).
> 
> All those risk levels are out there for you to experience, and we encourage you to really understand that and hold a portfolio that takes risk but does not allow for 6-7 at all. Even an unlevered portfolio is shockingly levered when you consider your future liabilities of paying for your lifestyle and your family’s education etc.
> 
> ***Leverage versus risk***
> 
> Leverage is a bad word. If you say that you are going to borrow money to invest, people will be concerned. These same people will also buy a house with a reasonable mortgage and think nothing of it. They will own concentrated stock portfolios with 40% annual price volatility when you are talking about leveraging a long index fund position at 1.5x.
> 
> The important part of portfolio management is having a portfolio with the risk that you wish and the holdings you want to own. The way we start is to build a portfolio of assets we want. Then we back test the range of possible future prices for that portfolio. As mentioned above, the measures that really matter are volatility and drawdown, although there are others. Volatility is the up and down swings over a measurement period. We use a few measurement periods, including daily, weekly, monthly, and annually. Drawdown is the size of a fall in a portfolio from any point to a lower point. It is important because when investors are in drawdown, emotions can affect judgement. We want to plan to avoid drawdowns that would force us to sell to ease the pain. This aspect of risk is important when we discuss leverage because of something called a margin call.
> 
> Ok, so we have the portfolio we want, but it is too volatile for us. It either swings too rapidly or has historic drawdowns we do not like. The upside of a more volatile portfolio is that its “expected” return is higher. But if it is too risky, it must be deleveraged. How does one deleverage? Simple: sell a slice of the portfolio until the risk is to your liking. Remember to sell each thing you have proportional to its size in your portfolio. Done! Now you have the portfolio that maximizes your return, expresses your investment view and you can live with the risk.
> 
> We started with needing to deleverage (keep some cash holdings) in a portfolio to meet your risk objectives to show that it is the risk objectives that matter most. But there are many portfolios that one can hold that do not have enough risk even if every penny of cash is spent. For us, DS Beta looks like that sort of portfolio. It is pretty low risk and, at best, barely meets our risk target. But for you it may be something completely different. The way to maximize return, express your view, and still maintain your risk level is through leverage. Put simply, that is buying more assets than you have cash to pay for. Oh boy. Now we must be cautious.
> 
> Many forms of leverage in the financial markets can result in margin calls that disrupt one’s portfolio and require risk trimming, which is not part of the investment strategy. However, before we jump into security selection as a method of handling margin calls, let us step back up. If you have designed your portfolio to meet your risk goals, you should have structured the portfolio so that the possibility of a margin call is extremely remote.

**So what**

Okay so lets agree that our first goal is to establish a risk target. Our next step is to choose a portfolio that achieves our risk target. We can do that by buying

- a risky portfolio with no direct leverage that meets our target
- a lower risk portfolio than our target that has enough risk that we can cheaply and modestly leverage the portfolio to achieve our target
- a very high risk portfolio with no direct leverage that exceeds our target but use less than 100% of our investment savings (hold some cash)

But the big takeaway is to always own beta and it should be really cheap to own.

**What is cheap leverage.**

Most of you have access to leverage but it is extremely expensive. The biggest brokers absolutely rip off their clients on margin interest costs.

- **Fidelity:** A high base rate of about 11.075%, but with tiered reductions for larger debit balances, dropping to as low as 8% for very large loans.
- **Charles Schwab:** A base rate of about 12.075% that decreases with larger debit balances, dropping to 10.575% for balances over $100,000.
- **E\*TRADE:** Around 11.45%.
- **Vanguard:** Around 12.25%.

You can not possibly use these margins to leverage up a lower risk beta portfolio to your desired risk. In the embedded twitter thread I describe some alternative leverage sources including buying long dated deep in the money call options that reduced margin costs to something more like institutional rates of 4.5-5.5%. But this is complicated and you may not be able to do this given your experience and knowledge.

**What do most people pay for Beta?**

Professional High Net Worth Money Management fees tend to be about 85bp. The market standard for “active” spx benchmarked is between 50-100bp per year for most ETF’s. Compared to 9bp for SPY its a joke. Compared to 60/40 of SPY/Bond Benchmark of perhaps 12bp its a joke. Buying deep ITM long term calls on SPY is also dirt cheap form of leverage for most investors. Obviously for more sophisticated investors using other tools like futures or box spreads can provide cheap leverage to reach risk target. There are a few legitimate justifications for paying these fees.

- Professional high net worth money managers provide other services including estate planning, tax optimization, and in the best of circumstances know your client skills which may keep you out of trouble.
- The investment manager may have alpha. If the investment manager is able to beat markets thats worth paying for. As many hedge fund managers charge fees of 2% directly and perhaps another 2% expected cost per year on performance finding alpha at 75bp is an absolute bargain.

But mostly paying high fees “outperformance” is a ripoff vs what you can do yourself because.

- Leveraged beta is not alpha
- Most folks who claim to have alpha likely don’t

**Is Leveraged Beta good?**

Finally lets get to the point. Leveraged beta that is cheap and transparent is perfectly fine and a real help that deserves a modest fee payment by investors who are unable to build it on their own. **That’s the big takeaway.** Even subscription services who provide beta are perfectly fine as most subscription services that provide beta are cheap relative to professional beta managers and can democratize investment information and education to a wider range of audiences and also help those who are already paying high fees to their registered investment advisors to have better questions about the cost and service these advisors are providing.

**But here’s the big problem with Leveraged Beta.**

While honest low cost leveraged beta is a legitimate service. Very very few if any investment managers, financial advisors, and subscription service sellers admit they are simply providing leveraged beta. They claim they are providing alpha. They justify the highish but not awful fees they charge by claiming and edge. In particular in every single year when the SPX exceeds the return on cash (most) every single leveraged beta provider should beat the index. They beat due to leverage (either risky versions of SPX or financial leverage via futures, margin, or options selling) then they brag that they have alpha. Very very few have anything more than luck.

BUT thats all fine. What’s the problem with leveraged beta masquerading as alpha is that outperformance results in return chasing. Return chasing alpha is one thing. Return chasing leveraged beta is something completely different.

Perhaps the greatest example of expensive leveraged beta done with absolutely no direct financial leverage but with massive risk taking to beat the index over the last decade is ARKK innovation.

Conclusions

- Everyone should always own beta at their desired risk target.
- Cheap leveraged beta is a real benefit to those with fairly high risk targets and poor access to low cost modest financial leverage.
- Leveraged Beta is really easy for financial professionals to create at very low costs
- Don’t pay financial professionals for leveraged beta much at all
- If you see outperformance by a financial professional vs the SPX your prior should NOT be that they have alpha but they simply have leveraged beta. NO need to chase leveraged beta “outperformance” and certainly no reason to pay up to those who provide it.
- If a financial professional is bragging about their outperformance of SPX it is highly likely that they were leveraged beta. If they are claiming they have alpha and don’t accept that they may have simply been leveraged beta, were lucky, or deny they were leveraged beta run away as fast as you can.
- Pay for alpha but its super hard to have and almost impossible for you to find. But red flags for those without alpha are super easy to identify. The biggest red flag is the strength of their conviction that they have Alpha.
