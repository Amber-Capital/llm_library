**Introduction**

Much has been written about passive flows and the resultant claims that “the stock market is broken”. This sort of thinking is lazy. Of course one must examine all of the supply and demand in a market to assess whether the price today is attractive or not. Markets are also forward looking so of course one must assess what future supply and demand looks like. Perhaps most importantly one must assess what factors will drive a change in behavior by buyers and sellers. Finally because every asset purchase has an equal and offsetting asset sale one must assess where the buyer gets the money to make the purchase and what the seller does with the proceeds of their sale.

Tl;dr today a strong U.S. economy is generating excess savings (buyers of assets) relative to the creation of assets (sellers of assets) resulting in assets appreciating more rapidly than in “normal” periods and substanially more rapidly than in periods in which savers are experiencing economic pain and must dissave while issuers are also in need of financing and must issue new assets.

Dampedspring.com members and clients receive detailed analysis of the entire supply and demand for financial assets as part of the biweekly Damped Spring Report and trades based on what the synthesis of that process determines. Today we at DSR see an imbalance of Asset demand vs Asset supply which is abnormal. That abnormal condition has inflated asset prices which has in turn inflated the real economy. The real economy is unlikely to “soft land” at desired levels of maximum employment and inflation at target. The synthesis of this current reality is that forward returns are likely to favor risky assets over cash and cash over bonds. However as markets are forward looking and Higherer for Longerer island is now consensus paying very careful attention to the supply and demand for assets in general and risky assets in particular requires knowing where to look.

In this article I will attempt to describe the broad picture of supply and demand for assets and equities in particular with a simple goal. There will be no answers for short term or even medium term trading while many readers may be interested in whether the stock market is going up or down in the next three months that is pretty irrelevant to understanding markets in a way that can be useful for decades.

I want to distinguish between the important and the bombastic. The passive flows that matter are a major driver of asset price trends. They are central to my investment signals. Indexing and target date funds are only vehicles for passive flows which may or may not distort individual stock or bond prices. Ill leave that debate to others.

Passive flows are simply income in excess of expenses (savings) being invested habitually by savers. They are price insensitive investments in financial assets. Savings are invested in financial assets. Every 401k or IRA contribution results in a flow into markets. As does any additional after tax savings. But perhaps more importantly expenses by an individual of corporation is consumption. Consumption by the private sector or the public sector generates someone else's income and ultimately the full amount of consumption creates savings for commodity owners, workers, and corporations. All of this savings accumulates first in deposits and then is redeployed in either financial assets or real economic assets. A large share of total savings is invested passively. Two takeaways from this paragraph should be

1. The size of passive flows is directly linked to aggregate consumption or in other words Nominal GDP.
2. A large portion of savers invest passively by “habit”. Habits rarely break. What would cause a change in habit?

**Where do savings come from - The Government “circle of life”**

When the government runs a deficit, it injects savings into the economy through spending and withdraws savings from the economy via issuance of government obligations (bills, notes, and bonds) because the recipients of the spending don’t immediately, or perhaps ever, save the money, but instead consume. An increase in government spending result in higher Nominal GDP all else being equal. That consumption winds its way back to savers who then buy the obligations used to create the spending. Tax increases operate the opposite way. Increasing taxes allows the Government to withdraw savings from the private sector which is returned to the private sector via the maturities of no longer needed by the Government. Tax increases reduce consumption short term until the savers get their maturies and they are now able to consume if desired. This is the circle of life of how the Government impacts the economy and markets. This circle of life can also be interupted if the Federal Reserve pursues quantative easing or tightening by either competing with savers to buy assets or selling assets to savers that arent receiving extra flows from consumption.

**“Who don’t need no stinking badges”. The private sector can create savings.**

The real economy does not need a budget deficit to grow. What it needs is private sector debt growth to match GDP growth. Real GDP growth is driven by two things.

1. Population growth - really the growth in workers
2. Productivity growth - the improvement of the ability of a worker to produce.

Because a new worker (and the more productive work force) receives a wage that must result in the economy growing its debt/money supply eventually. This may take time and that time depends on concepts like velocity of money but over time private sector debt growth and GDP track closely. Of course along the path of debt growth in line with GDP growth animal spirits of fear and greed result in debt increasing faster than GDP and creating an economic boom or decreasing faster than GDP creating an economic bust.

Let's look at the market portfolio which is all financial assets and real assets on earth. A growing savings amount driven by growing real GDP results in two things happening to the market portfolio.

1. Appreciation of these assets
2. creation of real assets and improvement of existing real assets

2\. is building/improving a factory or business that takes capital investment which may come directly from savings or indirectly from issuance of financial assets which are bought with savings. So Real GDP drives savings which drives investment which drives appreciation and real investment. All this is happening while financial institutions balance sheets grow along with real GDP. Of course cycles happen when either the public sector is printing money faster/slower than GDP or private sector banks are doing the same. In addition the Real GDP trend and its wiggles can change over time. That's important because if real gdp is hot it tends to create more savings than new real investment and that causes real appreciation in financial assets. So the point is GDP growth shifts literally changes the savings creation and the passive flows that set the trend for assets.

Two takeaways from this section

1. GDP Growth trends but is subject to faster or slower private and public sector debt growth
2. GDP growth is savings growth and asset growth and higher GDP growth can increase passive inflows from savers while borrowers (asset creators) need less money

We remain in an environment where passive flows are strong. People are earning and saving and consuming and that becomes someone else's savings. In addition government spending is still positive but slowing which is important as it needs to grow along with GDP in order to support asset growth as once the spending occurs it then gets offset with the issuance of government debt. That dynamic is the same with banks. They aren't levering with GDP. They are levering slower.

Takeaways in this paragraph

1. To understand passive flows one needs to understand government spending shifts and private sector debt creation shifts
2. Both government issuance and private sector asset creation is an offset to passive flows.

**Risky assets or all assets?**

Savings are saved in the market portfolio. The market portfolio consists of all financial and real assets on earth. In circumstances where savings and assets supply and demand are out of balance, and savings are desired more than assets are supplied, assets across the entire market portfolio appreciate. The reason is more about elasticity than quantity. What I mean is that at any instant all of the market portfolio is owned by someone. To the extent that a new marginal buyer enters the market due to savings and particularly when their entry is habitual and passive they must pay up to cause an existing holder to dissave and hold a bank deposit OR for a new seller to enter the market to borrow money for purposes of consumption or real investment.

So assets broadly are bid up by passive savings absent the need for issuance. The connection to the real economy is that a bidding up of the market portfolio also drives GDP growth. GDP growth favors equities over bonds as growth is good for equites and bad for bonds. However that doesnt mean somehow that savings all squeeze into equities. It is critical to understand that Assets overall are bid up but because of the second order impact on growth equities (and other equity correlated risky and/or pro growth assets) outperform bonds. BUT Bonds can still rally.

Here at damped spring we measure the GDP impact separate from the flow impact. The conceptual underpinning of the savings demand vs market portfolio supply is best seen by measuring risk premiums. I have written a twitter thread on the basics of risk premiums [here.](https://x.com/dampedspring/status/1441760680386854921)

The next concept to consider when thinking about the supply and demand for stocks and all assets is what is often called the portfolio rebalance channel. I call it risk premium arbitrage. Let’s assume for the moment that all assets at this very second are held by folks who want what they have. Essentially at any moment supply and demand for assets is balanced. What that implies is there is not a single asset that has a higher expected risked adjust return than another asset. Meaning bonds and stocks have the same expected risk adjusted return. Of course bonds and stocks may have different actual returns based on their relative volatility and risk but that fact doesn’t impact their price in our framework as a first order input.

If bonds and stocks have the same expected return then the optimal way for a marginal saver to invest would be to invest exactly in the market portfolio bidding up all assets. What if the market portfolio was not fairly priced and offered a higher risk adjusted return for a particular asset. Our framework which we call risk premium arbitrage assigns risk premium to expected return and assumes over time that any asset that offers a higher expected return vs risk gets bid up at the expense of other assets. Of course tomorrow or at any moment future expectations for the other drivers of macro asset returns can shift favoring stocks over bonds or bonds over stocks or even assets over cash but at this very moment its risk premium arbitrage that keeps expected return in line and drives market portfolio returns.

Now remember we are talking about passive flows from savings. Takeaways from this section

1. We suggest that passive flows from savings chase the market portfolio with equal vigor
2. At any moment may not be true resulting in risk premium dislocations and arbitrage pressures.
3. Economic growth expectations are buoyed by asset inflation which at a second order level may influence asset allocation.

But once again we need to look at a broader picture of supply and demand.

**Supply of assets.**

Our focus remains on passive flows from savers. We have stated that, absent short term dislocations, that this inflow pushes up that market portfolio. What of supply. First its important to dispell one notion quickly. Supply of financial assets come from only one meaningful source. It comes from entities that want to consume or invest in the real economy. I imagine you are thinking about sellers who owned the assets and short sellers as suppliers. The amount of turnover of true holders of assets is tiny. The lions share of assets are held by those who buy and hold. The short seller is a tiny factor in most macro markets and is perfectly offset by a long holder. While many many investing strategies including active alpha strategies involved in long only stock picking to global macro impact the wiggles of markets this article is not about them.

The real supply of financial assets is dominated by corporations, governments and banks. Corporations issue equity and corporate bonds to finance real economy investment. Governments issue government bonds to spend or hand to the private sector to allow them to spend on consumption when taxes are unable to satisfy the spending. Banks make loans and dont necessarrily need to sell any assets to the private sector saver but often do by packaging loans into securities or mortgages into MBS.

The point of assessing the supply of assets is two fold

1. Each of these entities only create certain assets and can’t offer all assets
2. All of the proceeds of assets sales except for bank asset sales increase consumption which flows to savings.

But the big takeaway is the sellers of assets can not pursue risk premium arbitrage like the saver can. A corporation can’t tap the government bond market and the government can’t tap the equity market. This fact may result in meaningful distortions in supply which may overwhelm the risk premium arbitrage that exists with savers. Savers really have the entire market portfolio to invest in while the supply has silos.

In synthesis.

- Passive flows matter and impact the trend in markets
- Passive flows matter to all assets
- If passive flows bid up assets a second order impact may be support to real gdp and a preference for equities
- Passive flows track GDP and will decline and rise with GDP
- Supply is silo’ed
- A full accounting of all flows is necessary to assess current market conditions and economic second order impact.
