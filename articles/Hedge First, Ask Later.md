I’ve been a bit quiet since my last note.

Not because nothing has been happening, but because this has been one of those stretches where the market has become noisier than the signal. Every few hours another headline hits, oil and rates jump, credit gets dragged into the conversation, software gets used as a pinata again, and yet the market still refuses to give anyone the clean break they’re desperate for.

This kind of environment can easily fool people. If you only look at the S&P, it feels like we are just chopping around in a frustrating range. If you look underneath the surface, there has been plenty going on. Hedge funds have been cutting, ETF shorts have surged, asset managers have largely frozen while systematic exposure has been coming down again. ETF shorts just posted one of the biggest weekly increases on record, yet single stocks were still modestly net bought. I don’t think I would deem the flow and positioning situation panic - more of a hedge first and ask the harder questions later kind of thing.

So the right framing here is not "everything is broken." It is that we are in an ongoing positioning clean-up, and until the war path becomes clearer, the Fed speaks, and the market gets more confidence around the next macro leg, equities probably continue to trade with higher vol and a lower tolerance for bad headlines.

The part I think most people are still getting wrong is what the real fear should be.

A few weeks ago I told you the right tail was being abandoned. I still believe that. What has changed is the path to it.

So far the market has traded this like a pretty standard geopolitical inflation scare. Oil up, yields up, Europe pressured, credit nerves rising, and anything remotely cyclical or duration-sensitive getting smacked around. Fair enough. That is the obvious first move.

But obvious first moves are not always the right ones, and they are very rarely the final ones.

The real question is what happens if this stops being a scare and starts lingering. Because the longer energy stays elevated, the less this looks like a clean inflation story and the more it starts to look like a growth problem. At the end of the day, higher oil is not just a number on a screen. It tightens financial conditions, leans on demand and acts like a tax. This is when markets woudl stop obsessing over the initial inflation impulse and start worrying about what that impulse does to growth. That is when the correlation structure changes and the downside starts to make more sense. To me, the market has reacted to the event. I’m not convinced it has really thought through what happens if the event hangs around.

Let’s start with the on-going theatre, credit. There is stress, no question. Private credit is being tested properly now, gates are being used, redemptions are rising and some weaker borrowers are going to have a horrible time of it. But I think people are making the classic mistake of hearing “credit stress” and immediately jumping to “systemic event.” I’m not there. To me, this still looks more like a repricing of who ends up wearing the pain than the start of some all-out contagion cycle. Capital gets tighter, standards get tougher, weak structures get exposed and the market becomes a lot less forgiving. That is unpleasant, but unpleasant and systemic are not the same thing.

Positioning is still cleaning up, but let’s not pretend this thing is washed out. GS prime had US fundamental long/short gross down 3.4 points last week to 221.1%, the biggest weekly drop in four months, with net down 1.3 points to 53.5%. ETF shorts have surged, US equities have now been net sold for four straight weeks, and yet single stocks were still modestly net bought. That is not capitulation, it is the market starting to hedge first and ask questions later.

The important bit is that 53.5% net still is not especially low. It is around the 69th percentile on a one-year lookback, versus ~40 during Liberation Day. So yes, people have moved their feet. Long-onlys have frozen, hedge funds have reached for macro hedges, and asset managers have been forced into the futures market in size. But this still looks more like a reality check/tap on the shoulder from Mr Market than a proper flush.

Systematics are part of the issue too. Vol-targeting exposure has dropped from around the 61st percentile to the 47th in just over a week, CTAs are back nearer neutral, and the market is still close enough to trigger levels that more de-risking remains firmly on the table. Goldman’s model points to another $69B of CTA selling over the next week on a flat tape and $98B over the next month. So yes, the cleanup is underway. No, I do not think it is done. Also worth remembering that CTAs are trend followers, not heroic bottom pickers. If the market does start to turn before all of this is fully resolved, they can easily still be de-risking into that reversal.

On the Fed, I do not think Wednesday is going to give markets the neat little reset some people are hoping for. I expect no change, and more importantly I expect Powell to do what Powell usually does when the backdrop is messy, which is try to avoid giving either side a clean soundbite. He will probably lean on uncertainty, remind everyone that policy is not on autopilot, and make it pretty clear that the bar for reacting to an energy shock is different if inflation expectations stay under control.

The problem for him is that this is a very narrow fairway. If he sounds too relaxed, markets will hear complacency on inflation. If he sounds too hawkish, markets will hear higher for longer. Either way, someone comes away unhappy. That is why my base case is not that the Fed resolves the chop. My base case is that it adds to it. The market wants certainty and Powell is far more likely to offer conditionality.

In fixed income, the initial move has made total sense, but the bigger opportunity is increasingly on the other side of it. My read on rates is that the market is still trading the first-order effect, not the full one. Higher oil hits the tape and the instinctive move is to price it as hawkish, push yields up and talk about inflation again. This is fair enough. But if this drags beyond a headline spike, the story changes. What starts as inflation pressure can very quickly become tighter financial conditions, softer demand and slower growth. That is the part I do not think is fully priced yet. So while the initial backup in yields makes sense, I would not be surprised if the bigger move eventually turns out to be the other way. That is why I am not interested in chasing the sell-off in bonds here. I do not think this is the start of some sustained breakout in yields. I think we are getting closer to the point where duration starts to make more sense again.

Naturally FX is telling a similar story. EUR downside has been the clean macro expression of the shock, but a lot of that move has already happened now, with EUR/USD down 3.8% over the last month as I write this. Flows show EUR longs have been cut aggressively across both futures and options, taking aggregate positioning back close to neutral. Cable (GBP/USD for those who don’t trade FX) is totally different. The short positioning here looks much more entrenched, with futures already back to roughly 2 sigma short. If we get a flush toward 1.31, I’m probably a buyer. That said, the broader point still stands. The easy Europe-negative FX expression is not as clean as it was two weeks ago, but if energy stays elevated and keeps tightening financial conditions, ye olde playbook of dollar strength versus Europe can reassert itself very quickly. My read is that the cleanest part of the G10 dollar squeeze has probably already happened, but that does not mean Europe is out of the woods, yet.

On Iran, nobody has conviction on duration. That is the problem for equities. No one really knows the off-ramp. Prediction markets imply a ~50% ceasefire within two months which is effectively prediction markets saying it has 0 edge on timing - the same with traditional markets. Hormuz is not running as it should be, single digit % activity compared to normal. TTF is going to end up higher if Qatari LNG stays halted for two months. I think oil is overdone, very much so - like the VIX being above 30, oil does not live above $100. And when oil breaks, it is going to take a lot of the fear with it. Sadly, every headline for oil still matters because of vol being so heightened and the right tail remaining very real for now. When you lookback, one must say that selling ITM call spreads would seem to be the trade up here.

So I guess, like me, you’re wondering - where does that all actually leave us. My take - higher vol, more violent rotations and a few more false starts before we are off to the races again. But we are closer to the turn than most people think. I think index continues to look more resilient than single names and specific sectors. Because this is very much a hedge macro while staying selective within. I don’t think the positioning cleanup is complete just yet, but we are close. The setup for the right tail to play out is arguably better than it has been for a while. Shorts are crowded, systematics are near neutral, and all it needs is a catalyst. The war path desperately needs to clear up and markets desperately need that certainty. There’s simply too much systematic supply for markets to really catch a bid here - we need some kind of flush. If that is a quick move below the 200d - so be it.

The good news is that this does not look like a systemic unraveling. Markets do not need a systemic event to remain difficult. They just need enough uncertainty to keep people off balance, enough energy pressure to keep the inflation nerves (mmm borderline PTSD post covid) alive, and enough growth fear in the distance to stop anyone leaning too hard the other way. The right tail very much exists, it’s just not going to be as simple as I had originally thought.
