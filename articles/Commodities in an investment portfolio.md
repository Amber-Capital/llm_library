**Trading vs Investing - Alpha vs Beta.**

Whenever I approach a topic like this I attempt to break down the issue. In all my investment work the biggest single issue is whether I am talking about trading or investing. I consider these two things to be quite different. My preferred labels are trading is “alpha” and investing is “beta”.

Alpha is attempting to beat the market. Beta is investing over the long term in assets that generate excess returns over holding tbills or other interest bearing cash. I think Alpha is very hard and most of us can’t beat the market. Beta is essentially free money that is paid by those who want our savings to consume or invest in the real economy and must compete with all others who want our savings and must at a minimum pay more than the tbill rate to get us to take on risk.

I’ve written a twitter thread on that payment which is called “risk premium” here.

**Do Commodities belong in a beta portfolio?**

Bridgewater was revalation to me in many ways. But perhaps the most important was its thinking on what makes an asset good for a long term portfolio. I have built my own framework standing on the shoulders of Bridgewater, other great firms, and academic and practical thought leaders. Again this is basic and may appear naive. This is the point. Start with basics. Build from basics. Review basics on occasion.

In order to qualify to be an asset in long only passive investment portfolio ideally four things are true.

1. The asset collects risk premiums over time
2. The aset prodices unique and ideally fairly substantial diversification benefit vs other portfolio assets.
3. The asset can be acquired and held with low fees and little or no inconvenience.
4. The assets are liquid

Public Index Equities are a great asset to hold as they qualify in all ways. They are strongly pro growth. Nominal Developed Market Treasury bonds with adequate yields are also great as they qualify in all ways and they are strongly anti growth and benefit from disinflation. Inflation linked bonds are pretty good as they are excellent and very clean anti growth assets which collect risk premiums and are mostly pretty liquid but they are unattractive to hold as they can’t be leveraged easily and they have annoying tax treatement.

Using this framework I think commodities are an asset worth holding in a long only beta portfolio though but cutting to the chase they are a compromise. They have excellent portfolio deversification benefits and are pretty liquid. Unfortunately some commodites have no risk premiums, others have some small risk premium, few have adequate risk premium and a critical portfolio diversifier, gold, likely has a negative risk premium. Furthermore despite product innovations over the years commodities are inconvenient to own and when packaged into investment products high fee relative to other core portfolio assets.

Because of sample size limitations investors have to come to their investment decisions based on logic. Lets go through the logic shown above (which I agree with). Before I do that however lets begin to split commodities down a bit. I will break them down further below. But lets start with broad commodities and gold. Arguably you could include other precious metals in the gold category and I wll below. But other precious metals are more hybrid than gold. Gold has historically been a unique asset to hold. Its possible other precious metals and even crypto belong in that category but thats in the weeds.

Broad commodities are stuff that people want to consumer directly or are components in things people consumer or invest in when building real assets. People consume more commodities when the economy is growing and consume less when the economy is slowing. When monetary inflation is occuring (ignoring for the moment the normal supply disruption sort of inlfation or the very rare non monetary demand driven inflation) there is more money chasing all things as commodities are real physical things they respond favorably to monetary system expansion.

Gold has demonstrated throughout history to respond well to monetary inflation. But unlike broad commodities does not respond well to growth. While jewelery consuption may rise in rising growth envirmonments gold is mostly a monetary asset. Rising growth creates superior opportunites for investment which encourages those who own gold to sell and invest in growth assets. Furthermore in rising growth environments real interst rates rise so those who save in gold or fiat favor fiat as it pays a higher real interest rate while gold just doesnt ever have any yield. For the second reason gold performs well in falling growth environments due to a drop in the differential between its zero interest rate and real interest rates on fiat.

*Do commodities have a risk premium?*

As mentioned above to include an asset in an investment portfolio it should have an expected return over cash as it is risky. Without a risk premium why would one want to take on price risk for no expected excess return. I have mentioned some unique properties of commododities that make them portfolio diversifiers and that could be all the reason I need but the diversifing nature better be hella compelling. Fwiw Gold is acceptable to me simply do to its diversifiying nature even though I think that it not only has no risk premium but likely has a negative risk premium.

The nature of commodity markets in general is that the spot market is where those with a need to consume today meet those with a supply that they can’t store. Some commodities can be stored and we will cover this topic below but in general spot markets simply reflect the supply and demand of stuff that is going to be used immediately. The buyers need it and the sellers need to get rid of it.

Commodity futures have different buyers. While some buyers may be ones who need the commodity in the future for their consumption need say for example an airline buying energy futures. In general the buyers are investors who want commodity exposure and have no interest in taking delivery. The sellers on the other hand are almost always commodity producers who may determine how much seed they plant today or how much investment they make in drills or other means of extraction. Like the seller of equities, and bonds the seller of a commodity future needs to compete with others who are looking to tap investors and compete by offering an excess return over cash or a risk premium. For these reasons I think in general commodity futures offer a positive risk premium.

However, When one issues an equity or issues a bond they have the cash for years. that means the risk premium NPV is likely quite large. Commodity futures liquidity is primarily in shorter dated futures. While it is true that investors can roll futures perperually I think the odds of commodity futures having a risk adjusted excess return that is highly competiive with more traditional financial assets is low. Proving this is quite difficult but the logic suggests that this is the case. As I mentioned this article is intentinally naive and basic. This question is one worth of high quality research. *Broad commodities synthesis*

I think broad commodites are a bit of a compomise for including in a portfolio as they are a decent diversifier, a mediocre risk premium collection asset, inconveinent and expensive to position and have ok but not comparable to other mainstream assets. Net net. Why not. Furthermore its one of those things where commodities could simply have not found there sweet spot over the past 50 years and so given they dont have much negative aspects a small allocation makes sense.

*Gold*

Gold is unique (for now). It is storable. The marginal spot price is not between producers and consumers but between investors. It is such a unique diversifer working in anti growth and Inflationary environoments that it is really unlikely that it has a positive risk premium but I think it is essential. The 70’s early 80’s and the last five years are the reason to own an allocation to gold. Will it work in the next 5 years? who knows. That’s market timing (alpha) which we will cover later. But assuming no knowledge of the future would I own it given its diversifiing nature and its likely negative risk premium? Yep.

**Breaking down commodities**

The commodity markets are broken down into Agriculture, Energy, Precious Metals, and Industrial Metals. Within those categories there are sub categories. In the chart below I consider the things that matter to me when choosing a specifc commodity for inclusion in a beta portfolio. Ideally they would be a decent risk premium, low seasonality, a curve which is less volatile and doesn’t rapidly swing from contago to backwardation, neutral to inflation and growth drivers, low risk of supply shocks, and medium or high idiosyncratic risk which likely increases risk premium while also diversifying the overall portfolio. I also mention storability as in certain cases that causes unwanted volatility. These categories of drivers and risks are incomplete, the grading of these things is rough. The point is to catch the big things and for you to consider how to refine this framework.

These categories of commodities are also not comprehesive but represent the allocations that dominate commodity indices.

*Agriculture*

Agricultural commodities have high idiosyncratic risk as weather has a signficant impact on supply. Demand is quite inelastic as it is driven by demand for food which is very slow moving due to slow moving population. This high risk also encourages producer hedging which increases risk premiums. While sensistivity to growth vs inflation is balance the overall sensitivity to marco drivers makes agricultural commidities less useful for balancing a portfolio but is a diversifier. Livestock is less desirable as its price is highly impacted and thus correlated to grains prices while its general lack of storability and subsequent curve volatility is likely risk without reward for the portfolio.

*Energy*

Crude oil is highly sensitive to growth. Unlike Agriculture demand is impacted by growth much more elastically. While still somewhat slow moving demand does matter. Supply shocks are a substantial risk to crude oil though somewhat less for Natural Gas. While Natural Gas is less likely to have a geopolitical supply shock its also hard to store relative to most commodities resulting in small blips of supply problems having signficant impact. Seasonality is quite high which complicates what one needs to hold (more contracts across an entire year). While crude and distillates have active producer hedging which makes for decent risk premiums, natural gas is an extremely volatile commodity across multiple dimensions and producers hedge less resulting in poor risk premiums due to both the level of risk premium and the riskiness of the asset. The energy complex provides moderate idiosyncratic risk which is a diversifer and besides natural gas a decent risk premium. It is more pro growth than desired. Pro Growth assets with atractive risk premiums are plentiful so this characteristic is modestly negative for portfolios. Net net energy comodities belong in a beta portfolio.

*Precious Metals*

Terrible risk premiums but very stable forward curves, high storability, low idiosyncratic risk (which is a must given the terrible risk premium) add up to a huge need for a positive reason for inclusion. Thankfully gold delivers. Anti growth and pro inflation just doesnt exist anywhere. Other precious metals have all the properties that gold has but their high exposure to economic growth more than offsets their “money” like anti growth character. Essentially precious metals have too much positive growth exposure to be as unique as gold. Frankly gold is must to a portfolio but other precisous metals just dont cut it.

*Industrial Metals*

These are pretty attractive. Though like crude they are a bit more pro growth than would be ideal, their lower idiosyncratic risk and moderate supply shock risk with decent risk premiums and low seasonality make them an easy keeper.

**Index vs a basket.**

Index commodity products are not designed for our purposes. By and large they use weights that are based on liqudity and production. While those are fine choices they are not based on my framework. Furthermore the index blindly rolls to the lead contract. Again this makes perfect sense for the index but given seasonality and contango/backwardation situations this would not be how I would manage a passive commodity allocation. Additionally, the gold allocation is way too small. Thats easily manged via a standalone gold allocation but is worth mentioning. Lastly, passive commodity index funds are distressingly high fee.

Unfortunately managing my own commoditiy exposure is really pretty complicated, costly, and inconvenient. Conceptually it would

- Own a different weighting than the index, probably exclude cattle, natural gas, and non gold precious metals,
- Spread futures contracts to mute seasonality and contango/backwardation volatility
- Overweight back months to maximize risk premium.

Anyway the allocation to commodities is by and large good. Its only 10% of my benchmark DS Beta portfolio. Push comes to shove I chose convenience over perfection.

**Alpha**

Ill briefly discuss trading commodities actively to generate uncorrelated returns. Alpha is beating the market. Its not long term investing. Its very hard to do and you probably shouldn’t try. Commodities are just another asset class to harvest alpha. In this section I will just try to describe some basic alpha trades and what commodity subsectors are likely to be more appropriate for various strategies.

*Macro trades*

Trading commodities based on major fundamental macro economic drivers like shifts in growth and inflation should be done with commodities that have limited idiosyncratic risk. Basically that excludes agricultural commodities. Metals both industial and precious are suitable for such trading. Crude oil has moderate idiosyncratic risk but that risk tends to be exactly the sort of risk that Macro traders understand. At Damped Spring we trade Crude and Gold for Alpha. Things we really care about include

- Growth and Inflation Drivers
- Geopolitics
- Monentum/Mean Reversion and Carry
- Positioning and flow of all market participants
- Technical analysis - given the high preponderance of TA usage in commodities TA is essential to at least understand what others may look at.

*Idiosyncratic trades*

Agricultural commodities are highly idiosyncratice and are good places to generate alpha is a trader has insight on weather, local crop dynamics, diseases risk, local country risk (particularly the softs) and even more rabbit hole dynamice. I dont even consider it. I don’t have a chance.

*Trend Following*

Of all asset classes commodities of all sorts exhibit strong trend following dynamics. There is alpha there if thats your bag. I limit my participation to oil and gold but the entire commodity complex makes more sense to diversify a trend following strategy.

*Crack and downstream spreads*

Crude oil distills into sub commodities. Livestock is just a pile of grain. Alpha traders buy the cheap version and short the rich version.

*Calendar spreads*

Have a view on short term supply and demand of storage of commodities. Curve trades may be for you. Ask Brian Hunter.

*Inter market trades*

Similar commidities trading on different exchanges. Should coverge? Be prepared to take delivery. Oops.

Commodities are a useful asset class and offer opportunities for both passive portfolio investing and alpha trading.
