**Here are the topics**

- Government debt sustainability in general and for the U.S. in particular
- The impact of the existing debt and future deficits on the current and future U.S. citizens
- The ability to pay the interest on the national debt
- The goals of YCC and QE in the context of monetary policy
- The Debt Monetization aspects of YCC and QE
- The Usage of YCC and QE for financial stability goals
- The conditions when these three different versions of YCC and QE are likely to be used

**Here are my hot takes on these topic:** Are they right? I think so but again I’m just a guy. Are they controversial. I don’t think so but lots of people don’t agree with me and have quite violent disagreements with me, so they must be controversial.

- The U.S. has no debt problem at the moment and is far from having one.
- The U.S. has a deficit problem which steals standard of living from future generations one way or another.
- The U.S. can pay as much interest as it wants on its debt.
- Stimulative monetary policy is executed by reducing relevant interest rates over the short term to encourage spending and investment with a goal of having the economy recover and having interest rates RISE. Rate cuts, QE, and YCC fail if interest rates are unable to rise over time.
- Debt monetization is a policy which permanently converts debt into cash reduces the amount of standard of living stolen from future generations and cause current citizens to feel the pain they have avoided in the past through inflation.
- Financial stability sometimes requires CB's to assume the risk of various assets from illiquid or insolvent entities
- The U.S. will use QE or YCC
	- for monetary policy when rates are zero,
		- for financial stability reasons when systemic entities fail,
		- will never monetize the debt because it will create pain for current citizens when pushing that pain to future citizens will always be preferred
- **Perhaps most importantly The U.S. is ultimately structurally unable and unwilling to cause sustained pain for its voters. Debt monetization is pain through inflation, Deficit reduction is pain from growth slowdown and unemployment. Neither are popular. Both cause pain in the lower 60% cohort. To keep them passive and not revolt neither deficit reduction nor hyper inflationary debt monetization will likely be chosen**

**Concepts that I lean on for my views**

**Standard of living theft**

I am just making this up and as I said Im not trained as an economist and that means I havent read many academic papers. So this may be very common theory. MMTers probably undertand it. But the most common misunderstanding about the government debt is generated from those who think about debt as a household or corporation might think of it. If you borrow to much today to consume you have to consume less in the future when you are forced to pay back the debt. Sometimes you or your heirs are bankrupted by your debt and experience a huge decline in your standard of living. Many think about the Government Debt as a burden that must be repaid. I don’t. As long as the US Government borrows in a currency they can print they can always pay back the debt. So unlike the household which can go bankrupt or has to save (reduce consumption/work harder) to pay off their debt the Goverment just has to print money. That sounds great but while its easy to understand the impact of debt on a households future standard of living its somewhat more difficult to understand the impact of government debt on standard of living.

I think of it this way. When the goverment borrows and spends it allows the private sector to consume beyond its means. However that spending is funded by private savers who buy the debt of the U.S. (I’ll come back to foreign buyers don’t you worry). One day in the future that excess consumption experience today which enhances the current citizens standard of living will have to be paid back via lower standard of living by someone in the future. The best way to think about that is via inflation. Basically in order to pay back the national debt the currency will have to be printed eventually and that will buy less stuff. Governments decide who gets the excess standard of living today and they will decide who gets the hit to standards of living in the future. But in aggregate our national debt is just a score card for how much standard of living has been stolen from future generations. Is it possible to steal all future standard of living improvements from future generations? Sure. Is it possible to ensure declining standard of living for future generations? Also Sure. Where are we today? Probably close to a point where future standard of living increase are nearing zero. BUT there is no mechanism for future generations to check this process. They don’t vote or aren’t born. Perhaps the only check on the theft is savers. They have voted for decades to steal from future generations but as they own our debt they are vulnerable to being the ones who get their standard of living stolen. The Bond vigantes are the only check on fiscal theft. You wonder why the Fed is constantly going on about long term inflation expectations? This is why. If they become unanchored it is driven by savers who don’t want to pay the standard of living decline cost and want out of bonds.

**The best way to stop theft is to stop stealing**

While many focus on our rising interest costs to service our rising debt amounts I dont much care. We pay our interest to our citizens. Our existing debt service costs are mostly just a small symptom of the theft. The big thing to worry about is our primary deficit. That has grown far faster than our interest cost. Each year our primary deficit layers 1-2TN on top of the debt pile while its taken 5 year of higher interest costs to layer 1TN of annual interest costs on our debt. Listen, neither are great but the interest cost is based on past sins. The primary deficit is an ongoing theft. Increase taxes AND decrease spending if you want to make a difference on the primary deficit. BTW if you do that the economy will slow and inflation will fall and interest rates will fall and that will address the interest cost theft as well.  
  
**The US Is structurally unable to stop stealing.**

The US economy depends on financial leverage and when growth and inflation turn into recession and deflation standard of living immediately decline and the fuckers who did this are voted out of office. BTW thats the good case. More often the long history has been that policymakers keep the economy cooking by deficit spending and easy monetary policy at any sign of stress. Voters vote for success not for failure and future voters dont count. Taking the pain can happen by accident but intentional pain taking just doesnt happen.

**Can foreigners cause us to stop stealing?**

Sure foreigners can stop buying our debt. BUT if they do they wont be able to sell us stuff and they will inflict pain on their own citizens today. The foreign saver is mosty just another version of the US saver and just as the US Consumer gets a standard of living boost from the US saver’s largess enabled by the government. The foreign saver including its central banks get standard of living benefits for its workers who make the stuff we buy. Anything can happen but if the Foreigners cant sell us stuff they will suffer.

In the end the solution for our debt problem is stop stuffing our faces on borrowed money. Its pretty damn unlikely we will do that but that is the solution. Lets leave theft and deal with the enabler of our theft.

**Monetary policy can spread out the pains.**

Remember absent a shift in government deficits the pain that is caused is felt through inflation. Literally our money buys us less stuff in the future and will buy even less stuff for future citizens. Central banks have decided that 2% inflation is a target. Why not zero? Well to achieve zero inflation they would have to cause demand growth to slow which would be painful for current citizens. 2% is a small bit of pain for current citizens. 4% is too much. The 2% target is what the Central banks are willing to allow. They are trying to cause some pain to current citizens and “protect” savers and future citizens but they have a limit. One of the best ways that we current generations could return some of the theft is by experience some of the future inflation sooner while we are still alive and the future generations are not born. BUT once again the burden fall unequally on society both today in terms of pain and benefits from the theft and its end and in future generations. so the choices are made by our elected and appointed officials and they are unable to keep their jobs if they cause enough pain.

That said monetary policy has been codified and approved by our representatives. We ease when unempoloyment rises and we tighten when inflation runs hot. We bail out systemic institutions when they represent too much pain for the voters. So far we havent and likely wont monetize the debt because that would be hyper inflationary and cause pain. Of course once the acutal pain is felt either by accident or intent all bets are off on pain sharing but thats not where we are today by any stretch.

**Monetary Policy Tools**

Monetary policy narrowly defined is not financial stability, nor is it debt monetization. Its using the short term interest rate for reducing borrowing costs across the yield curve to stimulate consumption or investment. That works until interest rates are near or at zero. At that stage QE or YCC is typically implemented to further temporarily lower interest rates to stimulate the economy. Some think QE and YCC also allow the government to raise debt cheaply and stimulate the economy via fiscal stimulus. Ill deal with that in debt monetization but as mentioned the amount of interest paid on new debt or rolled in existing debt is pretty irrelevant vs the primary deficit expansion when fiscal stimulus is necessary or chosen. If we wnat to stimulate fiscally and NOT Monetize the Debt we can issue debt and the interest cost isnt a big factor. With QE maybe we save 25bp. Who cares when your increasing debt at 7-10% of GDP. Anyway We will use QE for moentary policy again when interest rates are at zero. Any other use of QE will be done for financial stability reasons.

**Fed Financial Stability Tools**

Financial stability is an important goal for the Fed. The financial system is quite levered and an uncontrolled systemic delveraging can cause signficant pain for decades. The GFC was offset by many financial stability interventions and yet growth and inflation was below target for a decade after the fact. The Fed will absolutely use QE if it needs to offset the failure of an insolvent bank, or other suystemically important financial institutions even if interest rates are above zero. Basically the BOE did something similar with LDI when they reinstituted QE for a month. That was a measured and quickly reversed policy decision. We would hope that the Fed would do the same. Unfortunately in 2023 SVB crisis they acted too big and kept the programs on for too long and unlike the LDI crisis turned a temportary program to resolve a financial stability problem into a monetary easing. We don’t see systemic risk in the financial system at the moment but would expect QE to buy out distressed assets from a failed financial institution and would hope it would be more like LDI than SVB.

**Debt Monetization**

YCC and/or QE when monetary policy does not need to be stimulative is one of the reasons why some think QE will come back before interest rates are zero. The logic goes that our debt cost matters in some way and we need to lower interest rates that we sell our debt. Firstly as mentioned our debt costs matter very little compared to our primary deficit and secondly if monetary policy does not need to be stimulative monetizing our debt would be highly inflationary as both private and public sector seize on money that is cheap when money doesnt have to be cheap. That would absolutely hit some current cohort of voters with inflationary pain. Its an awful choice.

**YCC vs QE**

I’ll leave you with a narrow musing.

YCC is large scale asset purchases at a fixed yield

QE is large scale asset purchases with a fixed quantity at market yields

Except for Japan and in the US post war YCC is generally deemed inferior to QE for monetary stimulus purposes.

The reason why is that QE buys bonds at any price it is pushing yields down from market price. YCC doesn’t buy bonds when market yields are low and does buy bonds when market yields are at target. If market yields are low that implies that market rates are in play and thats when stimulus is needed. Basically YCC is for debt monetization while QE is for Monetary policy.
