There are three ways futures can be used: Speculation, Hedging & Arbitrage (Intertemporal, Geographic, Value Chain, Index Arb).

Futures are opened by either a long or short futures position, the investor becomes exposed to changes in the futures price and the position will incur profits or losses as a result of the movement in price. Holding the contract to expiry will oblige the trader to meet the delivery obligations. The price of the future is NOT the market’s perception of what the price of the asset will be at the time of delivery.

Investors who want to protect themselves against adverse price movements hedgeusing futures. Whilst hedging an investor seeks to protect a position in the underlying market by taking an opposite position in the futures market. A perfect hedge is a risk-free position. For example, a fund manager can remove their exposure to a stock market drop that will affect the portfolio of shares they manage. They do this by taking a temporary short position in futures in an index. This will deliver profits to offset the impact a fall in the stock market would have. Portfolio managers often hedge with futures as temporary shields against market movements. In reality, it is difficult to achieve a perfect hedge through exchange-traded derivatives, such as futures, due to basis risk arising from factors such as the standardised size of contracts and dates. Many people like myself will use OTC derivatives as with the negotiation of the terms, they are more flexible and we can tailor positions to hedge more precisely.

An investor who is long the underlying would sell the equivalent number of futures contracts to hedge the risk (a short hedge). However, although the cash and futures prices are strongly correlated, there is some flexibility in that relationship, which can also reflect market forces, such as financing rates. Basis changes will affect the performance of the hedge. This is termed the basis risk of the hedge. (Basis is the difference between the cash and futures prices).

Yesterday you learnt how to delta hedge with futures as well options. However, when using futures to hedge an equities portfolio, its beta is also useful when calculating how many contracts you will need to hedge. A share’s beta is the measure of its relative volatility. Therefore, a portfolio’s beta is the weighted average of the individual betas of the shares that make up the portfolio.

---

***Example***

If a portfolio manager’s port has a beta of 1.1 relative to the S&P 500, it means that if the S&P 500 index rises by 10%, that port would rise by 11.0%. In other words, the share portfolio is riskier than the index.

Therefore, if this portfolio has a value of US$8.0 million and the fund manager wants to hedge this portfolio using e-mini S&P 500 futures, the portfolio’s beta is useful in calculating the number of contracts required to fully hedge this portfolio. At the time the S&P 500 index was trading at 4200. The e-mini S&P 500 contract’s size is US$50 times the index.

Number of contracts = $8.0m/($50 x 4200) = 38.09

38.09 x 1.1 = 42 (41.9) contracts
