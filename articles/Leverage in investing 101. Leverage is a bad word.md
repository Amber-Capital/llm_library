Leverage in investing 101. Leverage is a bad word. If you say that you are going to borrow money to invest people will be concerned. These same people will also buy a house with a reasonable mortgage and think nothing of it. They will buy stocks with 40% annual price moves

---

The important part of portfolio management is having a portfolio with the risk that you desire and the holdings you want to own. The way I start is to build a portfolio of assets I want. Then I back test the range of possible future prices for that portfolio. My measures that

---

Really matter to me are volatility and drawdown. Volatility is the up and down swings over a measurement period. I use a few. Daily weekly monthly and annually. Drawdown is the size of a fall from any point to another of the portfolio. It's important

---

Because when I am down emotions can impact my judgement which makes me want to plan to avoid drawdowns that would force me to sell to ease then pain. This is really important when we discuss leverage because of something called a margin call

---

Ok so I have the portfolio I want and it is too volatile for me. It either swings to rapidly or has historic drawdowns I don't like. The upside of a more volatile portfolio is that it's "expected" return is higher. But it's too risky. It must be deleveraged. How does one

---

Deleverage. Simple sell a slice of the portfolio until the risk is to your liking. Remember to sell each thing you have proportional to its size in your portfolio. Done! Now you have the portfolio that maximizes your return, expresses your investment view AND you can live with

---

The risk. I started with needing to deleverage (keep some cash holdings) a portfolio to meet your risk objectives to show that it's the risk objectives that matter most. But there are many portfolios that one can hold that don't have enough risk even if every penny of cash is

---

Spent. For me DS Beta looks like that sort of portfolio. But for you it may be something completely different. The way to maximize return, express your view, and still maintain your risk level is through leverage. Put simply that is buying more assets than you have cash to pay

---

for. Oh boy. Now we have to be cautious. Many forms of leverage in the financial markets result in margin calls which disrupt one's portfolio and require trimming risk which is not part of the investment strategy. But before we jump into security selection as a method of

---

Handling margin calls let's step back up. If you have designed your portfolio to meet your risk goals you should have structured the portfolio so that the possibility of a margin call is extremely remote. Let's use an example. In the US one can easily open a margin account

---

That account with $100,000 of cash can control (for example only check your own rules) $150,000 of ETF's. HOWEVER if you bought $150,000 of ETF's and the portfolio value dropped a penny you would now have to deposit whatever loss you took or face a forced liquidation or margin

---

Call. If you keep cash in some other account to meet margin calls that already has to be included in your calculation of risk and leverage and expected return so you are deleveraged. BUT, if your risk limit portfolio is not $150,000 but instead was 120,000. The chance of a

---

margin call drops radically. If you lose 50% on your portfolio and it is now worth 60k and your equity is 40k you remain in compliance in your margin account. Meaning no margin call. Obviously that loss is 1.2x what you would have lost if unlevered but you were prepared ex ante

---

Let's jump into security selection and structure. There are a lot of trade offs when choosing how to lever a portfolio. They include

1\. Considering an alternative asset  
2\. Transaction cost and fees  
3\. "Borrowing cost"  
4\. Unwanted exposure  
5\. And your own ability to trade these

---

1\. Considering an alternative asset. The easiest way to add risk is to instead of buying what you want. Buy something closely related that is simply more risky. The best example is buying the 30 year bond vs 10's. Now clearly it isn't exactly the same. But is highly related.

---

Another example is buying a single semiconductor stock instead of the semi index. It's riskier but expresses the view. I strongly recommend avoiding leveraged ETF's for long term investing. They trade off badly on all my criteria.  
2\. Transaction cost and fees. All financial

---

3\. Borrowing costs. This is a big one. Know what you pay for your leverage. For a one year holding the benchmark "fair" borrowing cost is 1 year libor (I'm old school). ATM its 3.23%

---

If you leverage your portfolio with any margin loan, future, or option you should expect to pay 3.23% over the next year. HOWEVER, that's before retail markup. Margin loans from retail are notoriously high. They quote the rate on an overnight basis so the benchmark for comp

---

Is about 1.75% as the fair. Know your margin cost. Because using futures and options allows you to borrow at fair value and avoid the retail markup. The reason is that futures and options "borrowing costs" are arbitraged but those who can borrow and lend at fair value. This is

---

A benefit to you. Margin interest in a brokerage account is almost certainly a cost.

4\. Unwanted exposure. Many of you are able to trade options but not futures. This thread was initiated to discuss buying deep in the money calls for leverage. Here's how that works

---

Let's look at a 1 year call struck at 50 on a 100 asset with no divided The price of that call is going depend on two things. A. 1 year interest rate and B. Extreme tail risk. Because the option is deep in the money the tail risk may be fairly low. I'll come back to that. Instead

---

Of buying the stock for 100 you buy the option. That should give you a lot of extra cash which you can lend at say 3% for a year in this example. Let's say the option cost $50 you would have the exposure you want on 1 share and earn $1.50 on your cash. You would outperform buying

---

The stock. So obviously you need to pay up front for the interest earned. That pushes the option to 51.5 roughly. Then you also have only 51.5 of downside vs buying the stock and having $100 of downside. That is tail risk and the market is gonna charge you and it changes your

---

Exposure at a cost. Thus a trade off. The good news is it is likely fairly priced and the borrowing cost embedded is fair.

5\. Every jurisdiction and investor account has different ability to do each of these things. Understand your own situation and make the trade offs within

---

Your scope.  
To finish on the deep call. If you had DS Beta you could get to 120% leverage by replacing some of the TLT and SPY 1.2 more shares worth of deep calls. Then use the net cash you generate to buy 1.2 of the GSG, GLD and TIP. 1.2x leverage achieve.
