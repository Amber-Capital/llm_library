I’ve spent most of the weekend thinking about whether this move is actually the start of something bigger. My conclusion is pretty simple. I think we’re getting close.

Not close to some beautiful low where everything lines up perfectly.

Just close to the point where the downside starts to lose its elegance.

The bear case always looks cleanest once the market has finally started doing what it was supposed to do all along. Levels break, gross exposure winds down, systematics start selling, and suddenly everyone gets very serious. The same people shrugging off bad headlines a week ago start talking like the end is near. That transition is where things get dangerous for late bears, and I think we’re somewhere around there now. I won’t pretend I traded the pullback perfectly. I did call 6600, I had the core/satellite hedge and realised healthy gains on it, but saying I’m not feeling any pain would be a lie. After the performance post Liberation Day though, the lull was probably overdue. It’s a tough market to hedge right now. Options are too expensive, so your only real move is reducing gross and net, which is its own kind of blunt instrument.

For a while, this market had that annoying habit of absorbing everything just well enough to keep people confused. Oil up, yields up, credit nerves bubbling, software getting used as a punchbag, geopolitical headlines every few minutes, and the index just kept refusing to give anyone the flush they wanted. On the surface, it has felt like chop until the past week or so. Underneath it the clean-up had already started, and that clean-up is a lot more obvious now.

So yes, the market has finally started reacting properly to the stress that’s been building. That’s painful, but it’s also healthy. Markets turn once enough people stop fighting the tape. The bit I think people are getting lazy on though is that I still don’t think this looks like proper capitulation. There’s a real difference between a market that has de-risked and one that has fully washed out. We’ve had the first. I’m not convinced we’ve had the second.

This doesn’t feel like one of those moments where everyone came in barely hedged and suddenly had to discover fear all at once. If anything the opposite has been true. People have been well hedged and the short has built more at the macro level than in individual names. That changes what a low can look like.

Which is kind of my point. When everyone is already braced, you don’t always get the dramatic puke that people are waiting for. Sometimes you just get a drawn-out unwind. A tape that feels worse than it looks, and looks worse than it really is. The marginal seller becomes less elegant, not more aggressive. And eventually you get a bounce that nobody trusts because the market never gave them the cinematic collapse they were waiting for.

The underlying story here has been a rates and energy repricing that equities have been forced to absorb. And look, the inflation narrative gets complicated quickly when businesses can’t actually pass costs on to consumers. There’s no Covid cash floating around anymore. Higher oil on its own, fine. Higher yields on their own, manageable. Both together, while nobody knows whether the geopolitical shock fades or sticks around long enough to become a growth problem, and the market just can’t settle on the right playbook. First it prices inflation. Then it worries about financial conditions tightening. Then it starts wondering whether the initial impulse eventually flips into weaker demand. That’s how you end up constantly off balance rather than properly scared.

Could this have another leg down? Of course it could. If oil stays sticky, if real yields keep pushing the wrong way and if the market decides the growth hit matters more than the inflation impulse, you can absolutely get another flush. I’m not dismissing that. But I also think the easy part of the bearish case is no longer in front of us. A lot of damage has been done and priced in. The market is no longer complacent.

I’m not saying stop respecting downside, nor am I saying the hard part is over. I’m just saying we’re getting closer to the point where the next decent move is higher, not because everything is fixed, but because enough has finally broken to change behaviour. The market doesn’t bottom once everyone feels maximum fear in some abstract sense. It bottoms out once positioning has adjusted enough, once the people who needed to hedge have hedged, and once chasing the downside stops paying properly.

Not clean, but close. Market will remain sensitive to headlines and still vulnerable if rates and oil don’t calm down. But closer to a tradeable low than most people will be comfortable saying out loud while the tape still feels like this. Most people will wait for the all-clear. But the all-clear never comes until after the move!
