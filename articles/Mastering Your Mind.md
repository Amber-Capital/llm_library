Trading is not just about numbers, charts, indicators, or strategies. It's fundamentally a mental game. And the harsh truth of it all is that your toughest opponent in markets isn't the hedge funds nor the algorithms; it’s yourself.

You might have the best strategy in the world, but you'll struggle if your psychology isn't aligned.

Like my argument of size being more important than entry, psychology is more important than strategy. Why is psychology so vital? Because your own mind can either be your biggest asset or your worst enemy in the markets. Think about it. The market really doesn't care how you feel. Charts and algorithms are emotionless. The real drama happens inside your head. A sudden price drop might trigger panic, causing you to abandon what was a solid trade. A string of green trades might spark overconfidence, tempting you into reckless trades.

"The human side of every person is the greatest enemy of the average investor." Jesse Livermore

Understanding psychology is empowering. It means that by mastering yourself - your emotions, mindset, and discipline, you can find your edge. Unlike trying to predict every market move you can control how you respond. This post will delve into the essential aspects of trading psychology: I’ll talk about common psychological pitfalls that trip up traders, dig into the science of why our brains react the way they do, and cover practical strategies to build a strong, resilient trading mindset.

Some of you don’t have the luxury of a shoulder tap from risk management, but whether you’re a retail trader or a professional, getting a handle on your psychology can transform your performance so I invite you all to read this post. The journey is not straightforward but the rewards are immense. ## Common Psychological Downfalls

At some point in your trading career, you will face a minefield of emotional and mental traps. These psychological walls can turn a solid strategy into an expensive string of trades. The first step to conquering these traps is awareness. To start, I will shine some light on some of the most common “traps” and how they affect your decision-making:

### Fear of Loss

Fear is a powerful emotion – and in trading, the fear of loss can be paralysing. No one likes losing money, but when fear dominates your mindset, it leads to hesitation and poor decisions. For example, you might spot a great setup but freeze at the last second, fearing it could go wrong. This hesitation means missed opportunities: trades you should have won but never entered because fear held you back. Ironically, the fear of losing can also cause you to lose more by making you act irrationally. Something really common, and something I always receive messages about, is traders exiting winning positions too early at the first tiny sign of a pullback, grabbing a small profit out of fear that it might turn into a loss. In doing so, they cut short their winners and sabotage their profit potential.

Fear can also manifest as avoidance – avoiding taking any trades after a loss or avoiding the market altogether when it feels uncertain. While approaching with caution is healthy, excessive fear will keep you on the sidelines or stuck in low-risk, low-reward plays. Another way fear hurts is that it can stop you from cutting a bad trade, oddly enough. A trader might refuse to close a losing trade because they're afraid to take the loss. This leads to holding losers far too long, hoping they turn around, which often only deepens the losses. This behaviour is driven by a well-known bias called loss aversion – the pain of loss is so intense that we do irrational things to avoid realising it. Psychologists have found that losing a sum of money hurts roughly twice as much as winning the same amount feels good.

I can’t talk about this as some God, as I know one of my biggest problems with psychology is holding on to losers too long! The key to overcoming fear isn't to eliminate it (after all, some fear keeps you risk-aware). Something I have learned is to manage it. It starts with accepting that losses are a natural part of trading. Every successful trader faces losses – it's the cost of doing business. Instead of dreading them, plan for them. Again, it’s rich for me as I mainly trade with soft stops but use a stop-loss and predetermined risk management so you know exactly how much you'll lose if the trade goes wrong. When that risk is pre-defined and accepted, there's less to fear. Remember, your goal is to make good decisions, not to avoid every loss. By acknowledging your fear and preparing for the worst-case scenario, you take away its power to paralyse you.

### Greed

On the flip side of fear is greed – the insatiable desire for more profits. Greed is just as dangerous and, if anything, more dangerous. This is the most common problem professionals have. It pushes traders into overtrading and excessive risk-taking. Have you ever had a great day where you exceeded what you’d normally be happy with but thought, "If I made this much, a few more trades could double it!"? This is greed whispering in your ear, tempting you to keep trading even when you should stop. It often leads to forced trades on subpar setups, larger position sizes than your plan allows, or ignoring your risk rules because you feel invincible. The result? Those extra impulsive trades can quickly turn a green day into a red one.

Greed also shows up as chasing trades. For instance, you see a stock flying and everyone on FinTwit is boasting about their gains. You feel that FOMO and jump in late, hoping to grab some profit. But buying out of greed and FOMO, without analysing the trade properly, often means you're the last one to the party – and you end up buying the top just before a reversal. Greed blinds you to warning signs and makes you deaf to that rational voice saying "this doesn't fit my strategy." Instead of methodically following your plan/strategy, greed has you trading on impulse and excitement.

Another aspect of greed is not knowing when to take a profit, this one I hear about a lot - I honestly think learning when to take a gain is the hardest thing about trading, my TLDR, best advice for when to take a profit is “hold it longer than you think you should” but that never really answers it. It's letting a good trade turn bad because you kept holding out for a bit more. Every trader knows the pain of watching an unrealised profit evaporate because they got too greedy and didn't stick to their exit plan. Greed tricks you into thinking the market will remain in your favour forever. Of course, markets move in waves – what goes up will eventually come back down. Failing to lock in gains or moving your stop because of greed can wipe out hours, days, weeks and months of good work.

To combat greed, practice continued discipline and contentment with your plan. Set realistic profit targets (daily profit goals if you’re a day trader) and honour them. If you hit your goal, consider calling it a day – or at least take a step back before deciding to continue. Remind yourself that markets are always there, and there will always be another opportunity tomorrow; you don't have to catch every last tick today. Use rules such as limiting the number of trades daily or sticking to setups with a proven edge. Instilling structure leaves less room for greedy whims to take over. Over time, you'll learn that consistent, modest gains beat the rollercoaster of boom-and-bust that greed often brings.

### Revenge Trading

Revenge trading is the nasty cousin of greed and fear that appears when things go wrong. It’s born out of frustration. Imagine you just took a bad loss – maybe the trade felt unfair (you were stopped out by a tick), or you’re purely angry at yourself for a mistake. In the heat of that frustration, a voice in your head screams something similar to the following, "I need to make that money back right now!" This impulse leads to revenge trading: jumping back into the market with the sole aim of recovering your loss as quickly as possible. Unfortunately, these emotionally charged trades are rarely good ones.

When you're trading for revenge, you're not thinking clearly, you’re just chasing money. The frustration and anger from the loss carry into the next trade. You might take a trade that isn't really in your strategy, or you might dramatically increase your position size to win back the money faster. Essentially, you're chasing losses, much like a gambler doubling down after a bad hand. The outcome, more often than not, is an even more significant loss. One bad trade suddenly spirals into a series of bad trades because the focus has shifted from following a plan to settling a score with the market. But the market doesn't know or care that you feel wronged – it just is. Trading on tilt means your judgment is clouded, and you’re prone to repeating mistakes.

I’d struggle to believe you if you said you’ve not fallen into this trap at least once. The key to avoiding turning one loss into a meltdown is to catch yourself in the act. The moment you notice that you’re trading out of annoyance or desperation to get back at the market, walk away. Take a breather. Step away from the screen if you must. Go touch grass. Remind yourself that the market isn’t personal – losing trades happen to everyone. It's not about winning the next trade to erase the last loss; it's about making the next trade a good trade. Some fast money traders implement rules like: after two consecutive losing trades, take a 30-minute break, or if a single loss exceeds a certain amount, step away for the rest of the day. These rules exist to break the revenge cycle. As I always say, the market will be there tomorrow. It's better to clear your head and come back fresh rather than fight a battle in the heat of the moment that you're likely to lose.

### Overconfidence

Don’t get me wrong, confidence in trading is great. However, overconfidence is a real recipe for trouble. After a winning streak or a big trade, it's natural to feel on top of the world. Your analysis has been spot on, your book is growing, and you start to believe you've cracked the code to the markets. This is when overconfidence can creep in and whisper in your ear that you’re unstoppable. Suddenly, the carefully calculated risks you used to take might seem too conservative. You might start upping your size because, hey, you've been right so far – why not double the trade size? You might skip doing the thorough analysis you usually do, convinced that your intuition is enough. Maybe you break your own rules, subconsciously thinking, “This time, I know it's going to work, so I can forget about my rules just this one off."

Five steps forward, five back - Sadly, the market has a way of humbling us all. Winning streaks can create a false sense of security and lead to reckless trades. One common scenario: a fast money trader has a string of green trades, then sizes up into the next trade (or few), expecting it to continue, only to suffer a loss that wipes out most of the prior gains. Overconfidence can also manifest as ignoring risk management. For instance, not bothering with stop-losses because "this trade can't possibly fail" or concentrating too much of your capital on one idea. It may even lead to overtrading – feeling like every idea you have will be a winner, so you jump into multiple positions without the usual due diligence.

The antidote to overconfidence is staying humble and grounded no matter the gain (or loss). Remind yourself frequently: even the best traders in the world have losing trades. Treat every trade with equal respect, assessing the risk as if it could be a loser (because it absolutely could). Stick to your routine and checklist, especially after significant gains. Some disciplined participants intentionally reduce their position size after a series of gains, precisely to counteract any overconfidence bias creeping in. Recognise that streaks come and go, but what keeps you profitable long-term is a consistent approach. Celebrate your successes, and you should enjoy the fruits of good trading, but don't let winners inflate your ego. The goal is confidence in your strategy, not in feeling invincible. Markets can change anytime, and staying humble means you'll be prepared for when they do.

### Analysis Paralysis

Have you ever stared at a trade setup for so long, weighing every indicator and piece of info that you have, that by the time you decide, the opportunity is gone? That's analysis paralysis in action. It’s wise to do your homework, but it's possible to overthink to the point of inaction. This usually stems from a fear of being wrong – you seek that one extra confirmation, that last bit of info to assure you the trade is a sure thing. The irony is, there's no such thing as a sure thing in markets, and you know this. By over-analysing, you delay until the odds actually worsen (or the trade moves without you). You end up missing out on many good trades because something wasn’t perfectly aligned.

Nowadays, traders suffering from analysis paralysis often feel they need to monitor an overwhelming number of indicators: price action, volume, RSI, MACD, moving averages, Fibonacci levels, news feeds, economic data – the list is endless. While analysis is essential, too much information can easily cloud your judgment. One indicator says buy, another says sell, and a news headline suggests caution, but positioning says it’s a buy – and you become like a deer in headlights. Meanwhile, the trading opportunity that was initially clear has now passed or become less attractive. Overthinking can also mean you question your own strategy constantly. After a couple of losses, you might start tweaking and second-guessing your strategy, adding more filters and signals until it's so complicated that no trade ever meets all the criteria. This is a slow death for a trader’s progress, PnL and career.

To overcome analysis paralysis, I know I say it so much to subscribers: simplify!!! (and trust your strategy + gut). Strip your strategy down to the most important factors (the ones that truly give you an edge) and commit to those. Accept that you'll never have 100% certainty. Trading is a game of probabilities, not certainties. At some point, you have to pull the trigger on a trade if the odds are in your favour, even though there are no guarantees. It helps to set a time limit or a checklist for your decision-making. For example: if your key criteria are met, and you've done a quick risk/reward assessment – then place the trade without overthinking it. One mental trick is to ask, "If I was in this trade already, what would I do?" Often, that removes some of the fear of initiating. Also, remember that missing a trade can be as costly as taking a losing one. If you find yourself routinely watching winners go by that you wanted to take, it's a sign that analysis paralysis is at play. By simplifying your approach and being willing to act on your analysis once it's good enough, you can break free of the paralysis and start capturing those opportunities.

## The Science Behind Trading Psychology

Now that we've looked at the common downfalls on a practical level let's turn to why our brains behave this way when we trade. Understanding the science and psychology behind our decisions can demystify our emotions and help us manage them better. In this section, I’ll explore how cognitive biases cloud our judgment, how brain chemistry (like dopamine) affects trading behaviour, what the emotional rollercoaster of trading looks like, and how we can develop emotional resilience to handle it all.

### Cognitive Biases and Decision-Making

Humans like to think they’re rational creatures, making logical decisions based on facts. In reality, brains take shortcuts and are prone to cognitive biases – systematic patterns of deviation from rationality. In trading, these biases can seriously affect your decision-making, often without you even realising it. Here are a few major ones that commonly plague traders:

- **Confirmation Bias:** This is the tendency to seek out or favour information that confirms our existing beliefs and ignore information that contradicts them. For a trader, this might mean you have a hunch that a stock will rise, and you give extra weight to news or indicators that support that hunch while dismissing red flags that suggest otherwise. Confirmation bias can trap you in echo chambers of your own making, blinding you to real risks. It feels good to be "right," so subconsciously, we gravitate to evidence that tells us we are.
- **Loss Aversion:** We touched on this earlier – it's the idea that losses hurt more than gains feel good. Psychologists Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky famously quantified that a loss could be twice as painful as an equivalent gain is pleasurable.
- **Overconfidence Bias:** This ties into the overconfidence pitfall we discussed. Simply put, many people overestimate their own abilities or knowledge. In trading, you might believe you're more intelligent than the average market participant or that you have a unique edge for picking winners. While confidence is helpful, overconfidence can quickly lead to neglecting due diligence, underestimating risk, and trading too large – because you just know you're right. Most commonly, this comes after a string of successful trades, and it sets the stage for a rude awakening when market conditions change.
- **Recency Bias (this is relevant right at the time of writing):** Our brains tend to give more weight to recent events than earlier ones. A trader with recency bias might place too much importance on the last few days or weeks of market activity, assuming that the trend will continue and forget the bigger picture or historical patterns. For instance, if you've had a week of losses, you might feel that you're in a slump and become overly pessimistic about your strategy (even if it's proven over the years). Or, after a hot period, you might assume you've finally "figured it out" and that success will keep coming easily (this ties in with the overconfidence bias).
- **Herding (Crowd Bias):** This is the tendency to follow the crowd, believing that if many others are doing something, it must be right. It's human nature – there’s comfort in the herd. But in markets, following the crowd can quickly lead you astray, often buying tops or selling bottoms simply because "everyone else is." Social media can amplify herding; if you see a bunch of people piling into a trade and bragging about the gains, you might feel pressure to join, even if it doesn’t fit your plan. Worth a read - [Extraordinary Popular Delusions and the Madness of Crowds](https://amzn.to/4iMzE0s)

These are obviously just a few biases (there are many more), but they illustrate how our decision-making can go off-course.

Why do biases happen? Because our brains are actually wired to use shortcuts (aka heuristics) to save mental energy and protect us. In the wild, those shortcuts kept our ancestors alive. In trading, however, they usually lead to errors. Recognising when a bias is at play is the first step to correcting it. Something I do with every trade is trying to understand what the other side of the trade is thinking - why are people selling their shares to me? Why is someone selling this option to me? Why would someone be shorting this? If you find yourself only reading analyses that agree with your trade idea, start forcing yourself to read an opposing view. If you're hesitant to cut a loser, remind yourself of loss aversion and that minor paper cuts are the cost of doing business. By consciously checking in on your thinking, you can counteract biases and move toward more rational trading decisions.

### The Role of Dopamine in Trading Behaviour

**Dopamine addictions are real, in real life and massive when it comes to markets**

I’m sure we have all felt the rush of excitement when a trade goes our way or a strong urge to trade more after a big win. You can thank your brain chemistry – specifically dopamine for that. I am sure you know what the majority is, but if you don’t, it is often called the "feel-good" hormone because it's associated with the brain's reward and pleasure centres. When we experience something rewarding, like a tasty meal, a gamble that pays off, or yes, a profitable trade, our brain releases dopamine, and we feel a sense of joy, motivation, and even euphoria. It's the brain's way of saying that was good, lets do that more.

In trading, dopamine is a double-edged sword. On the one hand, the rush of a winning trade can motivate you to keep watching and trading – it reinforces the behaviour that led to the win. On the other hand, dopamine can massively alter your decision-making if you’re not careful. The anticipation of a reward (like the setup of a potentially huge trade) already starts raising dopamine levels, giving you a sense of excitement and confidence before anything has actually happened. This can push you to take trades out of excitement rather than solid reasoning. It's akin to how gamblers feel when they can't resist placing another bet; the possibility of a win is intoxicating, just one more...

When a trade does go well, and you get that favourable outcome, dopamine floods your system and creates a powerful feedback loop: you feel great and naturally (and almost instantly) want to repeat that feeling. This is where problems like overtrading and overconfidence often root from. Under the influence of a dopamine high, a trader might jump right back in for another trade without waiting for a proper setup just because it feels good to keep trading. It's not far-fetched to say that some traders become addicted to the act of trading itself, beyond just the profit, because of these brain chemistry rewards. In extreme cases, this can mirror gambling addiction, where the person is chasing the high more than the money.

On the other hand, when trades go poorly, it can cause a dip in dopamine and trigger stress hormones like cortisol. You might feel disappointment, which sometimes leads to that revenge trading cycle or anxiety about the next trade. The emotional lows of trading can be as intense as the highs, partly because your brain is experiencing withdrawal from that dopamine high and grappling with stress chemicals.

So what to do with this knowledge? Awareness is key. Recognise that your brain chemistry is influencing you. That burst of confidence after a win – remind yourself that it might be a dopamine afterglow making everything seem rosy. I always like to take a step back before rushing into the next trade, usually a walk does the trick. Some say to work out, but that itself also gets dopamine flowing. Also, creating rules (like a maximum number of trades per day or a mandatory break after X wins or losses) can protect you from chemically induced overtrading. By understanding that your urge to keep trading or the thrill you seek has a biological cause, you can manage it better – like an adult supervising an excited child. The goal is to enjoy the feel-good aspect of market success in moderation, without letting it drive you to bad decisions.

### The Emotional Rollercoaster of Wins and Losses

If you've been involved with markets for a while now, you know that it can feel like an emotional rollercoaster. The highs of a big win and the lows of a tough loss make your moods seem like a yo-yo. Recognising this emotional rollercoaster is important because it underlies many of the mistakes and stresses participants face. Let’s paint a familiar picture:

You start a new trade with optimism – you wouldn’t take it if you didn’t think it could win. As it moves in your favour, the excitement builds. Euphoria might even set in if/when it hits your target; you feel smart and maybe on top of the world. That’s the high. Now, suppose the next trade starts going against you. First comes a bit of anxiety. If it keeps getting worse, that anxiety can turn to fear – fear of losing money, fear of being wrong. Perhaps you hold on, hoping it'll turn around. But it doesn’t, and you finally stop out. You take a loss and feel defeated, maybe even angry at the market and mainly at yourself. If the loss is big or it's one in a string of losses, you might feel despair or frustration, questioning your abilities or strategy. That’s the low.

These swings are fairly common but can be dangerous to your trading if you let emotions dictate your actions. For instance, the euphoria from a green trade might lead you to the overconfidence that I mentioned earlier. The anger from a loss might lead you to give up good setups or engage in revenge trading. At an extreme, a trader can develop emotional burnout, where they're so stressed by the fluctuations that they start to dread trading and/or completely lose the discipline to follow their strategy.

There's also a broader "market cycle" of emotions that is often illustrated in trading psychology discussions: from optimism (at trade start) to excitement (as it goes well) to euphoria (at peak profits)... then to anxiety (as it comes off highs), denial (not wanting to admit a trade is failing), fear and desperation (as losses mount), panic (capitulating at the bottom), and finally relief if the trade is closed or hope as a new trade or upturn starts – and the cycle repeats. If you map these to actual market cycles, many traders buy in euphoria (near tops) and sell in panic (near bottoms), which is why mastering your emotions is so crucial to avoid being a buy high, sell low investor.

The goal is not to become an unfeeling robot; it's to keep emotions at a level where they inform you (e.g., a bit of fear can remind you to check your risk) but do not control your decision-making. ### Developing Emotional Resilience

Emotional resilience is the ability to bounce back from setbacks and stay completely steady during turbulence. With markets, this means not letting a bad day completely shatter your confidence and not letting a great day turn you into a degenerate. It's about maintaining a completely balanced and focused state of mind over the long haul.

How do you develop this resilience? I think a lot comes with experience – the more market cycles and trades you've lived through, the more you realise this, too, shall pass. Experiencing a complete variety of outcomes trains you that a single trade, day, week, month, quarter or year does not define your career. But there are also conscious practices to build resilience:

- **Adopt a Probabilistic Mindset:** One common factor of emotionally resilient traders is that they genuinely internalise that trading is a game of probabilities. They expect wins and losses and don't tie their self-worth to the outcome of any one trade. By viewing each trade as just one iteration in a long series (where their edge will play out over time), they don't get as emotionally hung up on the immediate results. This is a concept stressed in Trading in the Zone by Mark Douglas – when you believe in probabilities, a loss isn't devastating; it’s just one of the many outcomes within the probability distribution of your strategy. Likewise, a green trade doesn't signal that you're a genius; it’s just the statistically positive outcome. This mental framework greatly evens out emotional reactions. [Trading in the Zone](https://amzn.to/41AiKLj)
- **Self-Awareness and Emotional Intelligence:** Resilience also comes from knowing yourself. Pay attention to your own emotional patterns. What triggers your greed or fear? Maybe large position sizes make you incredibly anxious, knowing that you can just adjust your risk to stay in a comfortable zone - just because your book has grown doesn’t mean you need to rush sizing up. Perhaps a couple of losses in a row make you want to win it back; recognising that trigger, you can institute that rule to take a break after two losses or whatever you decide. By working out your triggers and typical reactions, you can prepare for them and respond in a healthier way. This is essentially developing emotional intelligence, something all the best hedge fund managers, PMs, traders and so forth that I know all have.
- **Building Tolerance Gradually:** You wouldn't run a marathon without training up to it. If at any point you feel overwhelming pressure or nerves, consider scaling back on the size front until you build more comfort. There really is no shame in trading smaller or slower; I do it a lot during a rough patch; the goal is consistency, not hitting it big overnight. In my opinion, this is something that comes with time. Every time you survive a challenging market or recover from a drawdown, you gain a bit more emotional callus.
- **Maintaining Balance Outside Trading:** Emotional resilience is also heavily influenced by your life outside trading. Proper rest, exercise (this is really important), and maintaining hobbies and relationships all contribute to a stable mindset. If trading is your life, every win or loss will feel earth-shattering, so get a hobby. By keeping perspective – that it’s just money and just a job, and that you have a life and identity outside of it – you avoid putting unbearable pressure on yourself. Balanced, happy individuals make better traders because they can weather storms without falling apart.

In essence, emotional resilience in trading is like building a strong core for an athlete. It might not be flashy, but it keeps you upright and strong when external forces try to throw you off balance. Even when you get knocked down, which you will, resilience helps you get back up, learn from what happened, and keep going with a clear head. One of the brightest hedge fund managers I know wasn’t some maths genius or any different from the pack. To me, he is a trend follower with a sexier job title really. Anyway, what he always stood out to me with was his insanely high emotional IQ. He was really really level-headed always, at work and outside of it. I have learned a lot from him and continue to.

## Practical Strategies for Managing Trading Psychology

### Mindfulness Techniques to Stay Present

One of the most effective tools for managing the chaos of trading emotions is mindfulness. At its core, mindfulness means being fully present in the moment and aware of your thoughts and feelings without getting carried away by them – it creates a space between feeling and doing, allowing your rational mind to stay in charge.

How can you practice mindfulness in trading? Here are a few techniques:

- **Breathing Exercises**
- **Meditation**
- **Mindful Breaks - this one I mainly do**
- **Staying in the Present Trade:** Mindfulness also means focusing on the trade you're in right now, not the one you missed or the one you're planning next. If you find your mind drifting – e.g., thinking about how you'll spend the profits, or worrying about wiping out yesterday's gain – gently bring your attention back to executing this trade according to your plan. Remind yourself: the past is done and the future is unknown. What you have is this moment and this decision.

By incorporating mindfulness techniques, you effectively train yourself to remain calm and centred amid market storms. You learn to observe your emotions like passing clouds rather than getting caught in the storm. This doesn't mean you won't feel anxious or excited – it means when you do, you won't be at those emotions mercy. A trader with a mindful approach can maintain clarity and stick to their strategy even under pressure when the market becomes volatile or unexpected events occur. By observing their emotional responses without judgment, they can avoid impulsive actions driven by fear or greed and instead make rational decisions based on their pre-defined plan which is a massive edge over those who succumb to every emotional whim.

### Trade Journaling: How Reviewing Past Trades Improves Discipline

If there's one habit that can single-handedly improve your trading psychology and performance, it's keeping a journal. Many groan at this idea – it sounds like extra work after an already tiring trading session. But the benefits of journaling are immense, even if it’s just jotting down emotions, trade, size etc. A trade journal is more than just a log of your trades; it's somewhere to write about your behaviours and a coach for your mindset. Writing down your trades and the context around them forces you to confront what you were thinking and feeling, which in turn builds discipline and self-awareness.

Here's how to make the most of a trading journal:

- **Record the Essentials:** After each trade (or at least at the end of the day), jot down key details. This includes what was traded, entry and exit points, position size, and the outcome profit or loss). But don't stop there – jot down why you took the trade and, importantly, what you were feeling or thinking. This might help identify patterns… for example, you might notice you often lose when you trade out of boredom or when you felt unsure but took the trade anyway. These insights are helpful in the long term.
- **Be Honest to Yourself and Don't Skip Losers:** It's tempting to gloss over or justify losing trades in your journal (oh, that one doesn't count because news came out, etc.). Be brutally honest with yourself. If you made a mistake, write it out. This is not about beating yourself up; it's about accountability. A journal keeps you in check because it's hard to hide from a written record. And don't just journal trades – you could also write about the missed trades or your emotional state that day.
- **Review Regularly:** Journaling is not just about writing; it's about reflecting. Set aside time to review your journal at least weekly. Most importantly, look for recurring themes.
- **Track Psychological Metrics:** Something I like doing is giving myself a simple score for each trade/day. I rate myself 1-5 on how well I stuck to the plan and rate my emotional state that day. Over time, these notes let you track your psychological improvement. Sometimes your P&L might lag behind your psychological growth (you could be doing all the right things yet still hit a rough patch). Cliche, but seeing progress in your behaviour should keep you motivated and confident that the monetary results will follow.

A trade journal turns your daily trading activity into data you can learn from. It came pretty naturally for me to do it as back in the day I was quite the sportsman… Importantly, it puts your thoughts and emotions onto paper, which often diminishes their power over you. Writing "I panicked and cut too early" in your journal is a bit like confessing; it brings the issue to light and makes it easier to address next time. Over weeks, months and years your journal becomes your personalised guide to your trading psyche, highlighting what you need to work on. Many traders find that the very act of knowing they'll have to write down a questionable trade deters them from taking it – a natural boost to discipline. Start journalling, your performance will thank you later on.

### Developing Your Own Mandate and Sticking to a Trading Plan

**What goes into a solid trading plan?** At minimum, it should cover:

- Setup and entry rules (I am not the best at this): Describe your trade setup. For example, "I buy when X indicator shows Y and price is at Z level," or "I short when a stock breaks below last week's low on high volume." Be specific. This removes guesswork in the heat of the moment.
- Exit rules: This includes both stop-loss (when you'll cut a losing trade) and take-profit (when you'll take partial or full profits). For instance, "I will exit if the price closes below my support level, or if I gain 8%, I'll take half off." Like nearly every option trade I put on, I cut 1/2 at 100% to cover costs. Clear exit criteria prevent you from hesitating or holding on due to greed or fear.
- Position sizing and risk management: Define how you will calculate your position size. I have a maximum position size but size position based on conviction. Some people like to have a fixed size no matter the trade…

Now, creating the plan is one thing; sticking to it is another.

Some tips to help stick to the plan:

- **Use Hard Stops and Limits**
- **Embrace Boredom:** Sticking to a plan can feel tedious at times. You might go hours or days (for me sometimes even longer) waiting for a valid setup, and boredom can entice you to take an unplanned trade. Learn to be okay with doing nothing when nothing fits your plan.
	---

One of the most liberating shifts in trading psychology is learning detachment – separating your self-worth from your trade outcomes. Markets have a sneaky way of making it all feel very personal. A big gain can make you feel like a genius, and a significant loss can make you question your intelligence or ability. To be a consistently successful trader, it's crucial leave your ego at the door and detach your emotions from the process. Trades are not personal verdicts; they're just probabilities playing out.

Think of it this way, heads or tails? Heads. If you flip the coin and it lands on heads, do you consider yourself a great coin-flipper? If it lands on tails, are you a failure at flipping coins? Of course not – it's just odds. With markets, while skill and analysis give you better than coin-flip odds, any single trade still has a dose of randomness. Anything can happen on one trade. You could do everything "right" and still lose on something as annoying as a Trump Tariff headline. Conversely, you might do something reckless and win by luck. The outcome of one trade (or even a handful) doesn't prove or disprove your skill. Internalising this is at the heart of detachment.

Here are ways to cultivate detachment:

- **See Trades as a Series, Not Singular Events:** A detached trader thinks in terms of the next 100 trades, not the next one trade. If you have an edge (say your strategy wins 60% of the time), you expect about 40 out of 100 trades to lose. So why get upset when one of those 40 comes along? It’s just part of the distribution. By zooming out to the bigger sample size, you don't ride the emotional highs and lows of each individual result. Think like a casino: a casino knows it will have some huge payouts to gamblers (losses for the house), but over thousands of bets, the odds are in its favour, and it will profit. The casino bosses don't freak out or celebrate each bet; they're focused on the long-term edge. You are the casino if you detach and trust your edge.
- **Avoid Personal Validation from Trading:** This is really tough, but remind yourself that you are not your trades. Making money on a trade doesn't make you inherently better or smarter; losing money doesn't make you less worthy or capable. It's just money, and the market is not judging you nor does it care, it's still moving irrespective of your feelings. One way to help this is to keep your lifestyle and emotional needs separate from trading outcomes. For example, don't start spending more after a large gain as if you've made it, and don't punish yourself or sulk in other areas of life after a loss. You can keep yourself level-headed.
- **Be at Peace with Yourself, No Matter the Outcome**
- **Find Fulfillment in the Process:** You can try to get your satisfaction from following your strategy, from executing a trade as planned, rather than from the profit itself.

Detachment doesn't mean not caring about trading or your performance, it means caring about the right things (process, probabilities, consistency) rather than ego or the emotional drama of wins and losses. When you achieve a healthy detachment, you'll find trading becomes less stressful. A losing day won't crush you because you know it's just part of the game, and a winning day won't swell your head.

### Visualisation

Top athletes often use visualisation and mental rehearsal (golf a common one, a player taking a step back doing their “pre shot routine” to visualise what they wish to happen and how before they take their shot) to improve their performance – and traders can too. The idea is to train your mind in the off-hours so that when you're in the live moment, you execute more calmly. Visualisation is so powerful because the brain, to an extent, doesn't differentiate between a vividly imagined experience and a real one. By rehearsing good trading behaviours and positive outcomes in your mind, you're priming yourself to act that way in real life.

This mental practice does a few things. It builds confidence – you've "seen" yourself do the right things, so doing them for real feels more natural. It also helps desensitise you to stressful events: if you've already run through a big market drop in your head while staying calm, you'll be more prepared when it happens live.

I can’t say I do it, but some guys I know use affirmations as part of visualisation: short, positive statements about how they trade. I find it a bit cringe if I’m honest, but if it works for you, do it.

It might feel odd to do this at first, but many who try it notice an improvement in their mindset. You're essentially training your subconscious to handle trading scenarios gracefully. Just as an athlete visualises making the perfect shot under pressure, you'll be visualising making the right trading decision under pressure. Over time, this reduces performance anxiety and builds a sort of muscle memory for the mind. When that real moment comes – a crucial trade decision – your brain will recall the rehearsal and you'll find yourself carrying out what you practiced, almost automatically.

Our conscious mind, the part that reads charts, studies indicators, and makes logical trading plans, is only the tip of the iceberg. The vast majority of your mental processing happens below the surface, in your subconscious mind. It’s the silent driver behind habits, emotions, and instinctive reactions, often overpowering rational thought when under stress.

Your subconscious is where deeply ingrained beliefs about money, risk, self-worth, and success reside. Maybe deep down you fear failure or feel unworthy of success. Perhaps subconsciously you associate money with stress, loss, or guilt. Whatever subconscious beliefs you hold, even those developed in childhood, can profoundly shape how you trade, often without your conscious awareness. These hidden patterns might explain why, despite your best efforts, you repeatedly sabotage your own trading success.

You must align your subconscious with your conscious mind. Imagine your conscious mind as a trader trying to steer a large ship, and your subconscious mind as the powerful engine below decks. If they're not aligned, the ship won't go where you want it to, no matter how carefully you plot your course. You can train it via journalling, visualisation and so forth.

### The Hidden Dangers of Too Much Screen Time

It’s easy to believe that watching charts and monitoring your screens constantly makes you a more dedicated and informed trader. In reality, excessive screen time often leads to poorer decision-making, increased stress, and burnout.

Spending hours glued to every tick of the market isn’t just inefficient, it’s really unhealthy. It puts your body under unnecessary stress, floods your system with adrenaline and cortisol, and mentally exhausts you. This chronic state of tension can erode your emotional resilience, making you more susceptible to the pitfalls I’ve discussed.

Moreover, overexposure to screens can degrade your physical health: headaches, eye strain (I have suffered this), disrupted sleep patterns, and even long-term issues like back pain or posture problems. And if your physical health suffers, your mental sharpness and emotional balance will inevitably follow.

The market is open nearly every day, you don’t have to catch every tick. Learn to set boundaries. Go touch grass, go walk.

Remember, beating the market isn't about watching everything unfold in real time; it's about making well-planned decisions and executing them effectively. Your mental clarity, physical health, and trading results will thank you for taking a step back. I really cannot give any better advice than taking a step back and going on even a short walk.

Set reminders every 60–90 minutes to stand up, stretch, or walk. Not only will your body thank you, but your trading will benefit from sharper focus and reduced impulsivity.

Give your mind and body the breaks they need, it’s important.
