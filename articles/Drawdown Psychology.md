"If you can keep your head when all about you are losing theirs…" - Rudyard Kipling

Every trader, whether dealing in stocks, FX, options, or futures, will eventually face the dreaded drawdown. It's an inevitable part of trading, yet when you're in one, it can feel like the sky is falling.

In this article, I’ll break down trade-level drawdowns and portfolio-level drawdowns (the peak-to-trough equity declines of your portfolio). I’ll explore the unique psychological demons each scenario awakens and strategies to conquer them.

You'll learn how to handle the fear and frustration they cause, and what tools seasoned traders use - from risk management rules and journaling practices to mental frameworks — to stay in the game. Along the way, I’ll touch on key behavioural biases (like loss aversion, the sunk cost fallacy, and decision fatigue) that make drawdowns so challenging, and share a few of my own war stories and hard-learned lessons from real trading experiences.

The tone is direct and honest, coming from someone who's been scarred by the markets and lived to tell the tale. Whether you trade equities, FX, options, or futures as a retail lone wolf or a professional with clients to answer to. The goal here is to equip you with a survival guide for the inevitable ups and downs. Let's dive in.

## The Psychology of Trade-Level Drawdowns

When you're in a trade and it starts going against you, the psychological pressure can be intense. Intra-trade drawdowns trigger a cascade of emotions and biases in real time. Let's paint the picture:

### 1\. The Emotional Rollercoaster of an Open Trade

Imagine you bought an FX pair (say EUR/USD) at 1.1000. Shortly after, it slips to 1.0950. You're now 50 pips in the red. Your heart rate picks up a little. You start second-guessing the trade: Did I misread the chart? Should I cut it now before it gets worse?

This is the feeling of a trade-level drawdown – that knot in your stomach as you watch an unrealised loss grow. Every pip against you feels like it's poking at your fear of being wrong. Loss aversion kicks in hard here: studies show we feel the pain of losses about twice as strongly as the pleasure of equivalent gains. That means a $5,000 unrealised loss hurts as much as a $10,000 gain would please you.

### 2\. Fight, Flight, or Freeze – Common Reactions

People typically react to an intra-trade drawdown in a few unhelpful ways. One is the "fight" response: you might add more to the position to lower your average cost (also known as "doubling down" or martingaling), trying to fight your way out of the hole.

Another is "flight": you panic-sell to cut the pain short, often just before the price turns (we've all been there – you exit in fear, only to watch the trade would have worked).

The third is "freeze": you do nothing, paralysed, or worse, you refuse to honour your stop loss because you "just can't take the loss." Freezing often leads to the loss getting much bigger than planned. This is where the sunk cost fallacy seeps in: you've already lost some money, so you irrationally hold on even longer, hoping to "get back to breakeven," as if that will redeem the initial loss. In reality, you're just risking more in hopes of not feeling like a loser. If you catch yourself thinking, "I'll sell when it gets back to my entry price," that's a red flag of sunk cost bias and anchoring to an irrelevant reference.

### 3\. Ego and Identity

On an individual trade, especially for experienced traders, there is often a battle with one's ego. You did your analysis and put on the trade with conviction – now the market is telling you (in the form of a floating loss) that you were wrong, at least for now. For some, this feels like a personal indictment.

The need to be right can override the objective decision of cutting a losing trade. You might move your stop further to "give it more room" (usually just giving it more room to hurt you). Or you avoid placing a stop at all, telling yourself you'll manually exit if it gets too bad (only to hesitate when that moment comes). Pride sadly has no place in trading, but it has a habit of sneaking in when a trade goes south.

### 4\. The Mental Toll of Watching the Screen

The minute-by-minute stress of watching a trade in drawdown can really take its toll. It's a form of torture by a thousand cuts – each tick down is a new cut.

You start imagining worst-case scenarios ("If it drops another 100 points, I’m fucked!”), which adds anxiety. In this state, decision fatigue sets in quickly; your ability to think rationally and stick to your plan erodes with each passing minute of stress. Neuroscience tells us we have a limited capacity for making disciplined decisions in sequence – after just a few tough choices, our self-control can evaporate. If you've been manually managing a losing trade for hours, your willpower tank is likely near empty, which is when impulsive mistakes (like a reckless all-in or abandoning your strategy) are most likely.

## The Psychology of Portfolio-Level Drawdowns

Trade drawdowns are the day-to-day scuffles, but portfolio-level drawdowns are the full-on battles that test your fortitude. A portfolio drawdown means your account equity has fallen from a prior high, and until you climb above that high, you're in a valley. If you trade long enough, you'll have plenty of equity curve valleys. Let's talk about what happens in someone’s mind during those slumps and why they're so challenging:

### 1\. The Weight of a Losing Streak

Unlike a quick hit on a single trade, a portfolio drawdown often accumulates over a series of positions. Maybe you had five losing trades in a row, or one really bad trade followed by a couple of small gains and then another big loss. However it happens, you look at your account balance and it's down significantly from its peak.

This is when self-doubt comes flooding in. You just start questioning everything: "Has my strategy stopped working? Was my recent success just luck? What if I never get back to my highs?" It's not just about the money lost; it's about what that loss means to you.

Many traders equate their account's equity curve with their competence or even their self-worth. So a 20% drawdown in the account can feel like a personal 20% drawdown in confidence. Indeed, drawdowns make you ask, "Am I losing my touch?" Our minds are pattern-recognition machines, and during a drawdown, we often see a phantom pattern of "I'm a failure" even if, objectively, a string of losses can happen to the best of us.

### 2\. Loss Aversion and Fear in the Big Picture

Behavioural finance tells us that humans are generally risk-averse when winning but become risk-seeking when losing – we hate losses so much that we'll take irrational risks to try to avoid or recover them. In a portfolio drawdown, this can manifest in two extreme ways:

**(a) The Panicked Turtle:** You become so afraid of losing more that you retreat into your shell. You might stop trading altogether or dramatically cut your position sizes out of fear. You pass on valid setups because you're traumatised by the recent losses. This is driven by loss aversion – you're overly focused on not losing any more, to the point that you stop executing your strategy properly. The irony is that this can lead to missing good trades that would have helped you recover.

**(b) The Desperate Gambler:** On the flip side, some traders react by doubling or tripling their risk in an attempt to win it all back quickly. If they're down 15%, they figure one or two big gains with heavy size will fix it. This is the loss-chasing mentality, and it's extremely dangerous. It's what Ross Cameron described as "emotionally capitulating" – the moment you say "screw it, I have nothing left to lose" and start taking giant bets as a last stand. That mindset can lead to catastrophic losses that blow up accounts. It's basically gambling with a tilted emotional mindset.

Both the turtle and the gambler responses are rooted in the pain of drawdown and fear of further loss. Neither is healthy for your long-term survival.

### 3\. The Sunk Cost Spiral

The longer a drawdown drags on, the more susceptible you become to the sunk cost fallacy on a grand scale. You've invested so much time, effort, and money in markets – maybe years of work – and now it feels like it's slipping away. Instead of stepping back to objectively reassess, many dig in deeper.

It's like being in a hole and deciding to keep digging because you refuse to climb out and start fresh. You might keep trading a failing strategy because "it worked before, it has to work again" or because you can't bear the thought that all the gains you had are gone. In reality, if a strategy genuinely stopped working due to a market regime change, the rational move is to stop or adapt. But in a drawdown, rationality is in short supply.

Traders often refuse to acknowledge that what they're doing isn't working, turning a multi-month drawdown into a multi-year one. Sunk cost thinking makes you hold on to hope and positions long past their expiration date, simply because you "have so much invested in them." It's the same reason a gambler keeps feeding money into a losing game – "I've come this far, I can't quit now."

Recognising this bias is critical: sometimes the correct (if painful) move in a deep drawdown is to hit the brakes, preserve capital, and accept that you need a new approach.

### 4\. Ego and External Pressure

A portfolio drawdown can be a public affair, unlike a private little intra-trade wobble. If you're a professional trader or manage money, a drawdown might mean explaining to clients or bosses why your book is down. Even if you're a retail trader, you might feel the need to "prove" to your spouse or friends (or yourself) that you're a good trader, especially after a hot streak.

A drawdown can feel like a humiliation or an identity crisis. If you pride yourself on being a winning trader, a big slump dents that pride. Ego defence mechanisms then kick in. Some traders become very averse to booking the loss and officially marking the drawdown "closed," because that feels like accepting defeat. There's also a tendency to isolate oneself during drawdowns out of shame, which can make things worse since you lose perspective.

Remember the stories of famous fund managers: even the greats like Paul Tudor Jones or Warren Buffett have had significant drawdowns or periods of underperformance – the difference is they acknowledge them and stick to their principles (or adapt) rather than denying the reality.

The point is, drawdowns happen to everyone; it's not a moral failing. But our ego tends to tell us, “If I were truly good at this, this wouldn't be happening." That thought is poison.

### 5\. Fatigue and Burnout

A prolonged drawdown is like a slow bleed. Day after day, seeing little to no progress back to highs can drain you mentally. This is where ego depletion (decision fatigue) can really wear you down. Each new trade during a losing streak takes a heavier mental toll than the last, because you're carrying the baggage of previous failures.

Over weeks, this leads to exhaustion, and exhausted traders make mistakes. You might become careless or start taking trades outside your strategy because you're just sick of being down and want to force a turnaround. Or, you become so gun-shy that you hesitate on great setups. Both are costly.

It's important to recognise the duration of a drawdown as a factor, not just the depth. Research has highlighted that the length of a drawdown can erode confidence just as much as its size. A 5% drawdown that lasts six months can psychologically feel worse than a 15% drawdown that you recover from in two weeks, because the long grind is mentally taxing.

Participants often underestimate this. You need to monitor your own energy and focus levels in a slump; otherwise, you risk sliding into a state of burnout where you either make a huge error or you just lose passion and quit at the worst time.

### 6\. A Portfolio Drawdown Story

Let's say a retail trader, Hound, built his $100k account up to $150k over a great year. He's feeling on top of the world. Then the market conditions change – say, vol spikes – and his strategy hits a rough patch. In the span of a month, his account goes from $150k down to $120k. That's a 20% drawdown from the peak (awful risk management, but this is purely fictional).

Hound is devastated. He hadn't experienced a downturn like this before. Initially, he reacts by halving his trading size, scared to lose more. A couple of trades win, but in smaller size, so the equity curve barely ticks up. Frustration builds.

He then decides, "I guess I just need one big trade to get me close to back." He goes heavy and over his risk parameters on a setup he's only semi-confident in. If it works, he could make $15k and be almost out of the hole. If it fails... well, he doesn't want to think about that.

Unfortunately, the trade fails. Now his account is $100k – he round-tripped an entire year's gains. At this point Hound capitulates emotionally. He takes a few revenge trades, swinging from the ropes (sunk cost fallacy fully engaged – he can't accept having wasted a year). When the dust settles, Hound’s account is $90k.

This is a drawdown far deeper than his strategy ever should have risked. It wasn't the market alone that did this – it was Hound’s compounded psychological reactions to the initial drawdown. This kind of downward spiral is sadly common. It illustrates how a moderate drawdown can escalate to a ruinous one if not managed well.

## Strategies for Managing Portfolio Drawdowns

Many of you might have had a rough year… If you find yourself in a drawdown in your overall account, here are some strategies and tools to regain control and work your way back prudently:

### Stop the Bleeding – Reduce Risk

You saw me do this recently!

The first rule of handling a drawdown is don't dig the hole deeper. This often means scaling back how much you're risking on each trade until you get your rhythm back. Many professional traders and funds have rules like: if drawdown exceeds X%, cut position sizes by Y%.

For example, one trader mentioned having a personal threshold: at 15% down from his high, he would drastically reduce his risk per trade, and if he hit 20% down, he'd stop trading entirely to reassess. This kind of rule is a lifesaver.

It's counterintuitive to some (because when you’re down, you want to make it back faster), but think of it this way: if you're driving and start skidding on ice, the last thing you do is hit the accelerator. You ease off and regain control. In trading, when you're skidding (equity heading south), trade smaller.

By cutting your risk per trade in half (or more) during a drawdown, you ensure that any further losses have a minimal impact while you troubleshoot what's going wrong. It also calms you psychologically, because you know you're not going to implode your account with one more bad day. Only once you've strung together a few gains or see evidence you're back in sync should you consider scaling back up. Think of it as trading with a safety net until you're performing well again.

### Set a Circuit-Breaker

Along with reducing per-trade risk, consider implementing a daily or weekly loss limit for yourself if you’re a short-term trader, for long-term participants, a set % drawdown stop out is a good idea. For example, you might decide: "If I lose more than 2% of my account in a single day, I'm done trading for that day." Or, "If I have three losing days in a row, I will take the rest of the week off." This prevents a bad stretch from turning into a meltdown.

Many professional desks enforce such circuit-breakers on their employees. It's rooted in the idea that after a certain amount of loss, your decision-making likely deteriorates (due to emotion), so it's better to step away and regroup rather than keep fighting the market.

Having a hard stop like this in your plan can save you from yourself. For instance, if you hit your loss limit by 11am one day, as painful as it is to walk away, you do it. You live to trade another day with a clear head. Remember, opportunities in the market are like buses – another one will come along (the markets there tomorrow, next week and next month ya-di-ya-da. **But only if you still have capital to ride with.**

### Review and Recalibrate (Don't Just Rage Trade)

When drawdown hits, it's time to open the journal and do a post-mortem on your recent trades. Go through each loss and look for commonalities. Did you deviate from your strategy out of frustration or greed? Were you trading an asset or a time frame you're not as familiar with? Was there a change in market conditions (volatility, trend, news) that made your setups less effective?

Often, a drawdown is trying to tell you something. It could be external – e.g. your trend-following system hit a consolidation period – or internal – e.g. you started slipping on discipline. By systematically analysing the losing streak, you might spot a fixable issue.

Maybe you notice you were trading without a stop on a few trades (time to recommit to always using stops!). Importantly, also review your mindset during those trades: did you make emotional decisions?

This is where keeping a trading journal with notes on your emotions pays off. As I said in my other psychology post, record how you feel on each trade – anxious, overconfident, fearful, etc. – so you can identify when those feelings correlate with mistakes. Use your journal to learn from the drawdown. Each loss is tuition paid, so extract the lesson. Otherwise, you paid for nothing.

### Reconnect with Your Strategy

A well-prepared participant will know what kind of drawdowns to expect because they've studied their strategy's historical performance. If you've backtested or have long-term records, you should know the maximum historical drawdown of your approach.

For example, you might know that over the past 5 years, your strategy had a worst-case drawdown of 15%. Armed with that knowledge, if you're sitting at a 10% drawdown now, you can remind yourself this is within the tested tolerance of your method. It doesn't guarantee the drawdown won't exceed 15% this time, but it gives perspective: "I've been here before (or my strategy has), this could just be the normal down cycle."

This can prevent the panic-induced strategy hopping that plagues many traders. On the other hand, if your drawdown is outside historical norms – say your worst-ever was 15% and now you're down 25% – that's a strong sign something is different/wrong (either in the market or in your execution). It calls for a serious re-evaluation.

Maybe volatility is way beyond anything in your backtest, or maybe you started taking trades outside the system. Either way, that's when you might decide to halt trading and reassess.

The point is, knowing your performance metrics gives you a baseline to judge the current slump. It turns an emotional "oh God, I'm down so much" into an analytical "I'm at 1.3x my expected max drawdown; time to adjust." It's much easier to stay calm when you have data defending you. As the saying goes, "If you can't measure it, you can't manage it." So measure your strategy results and refer to them when the going gets tough.

### Equity Curve Monitoring and "Performance Triggers"

A practical tool some traders use is overlaying a simple trend indicator on their equity curve. For instance, they might calculate a moving average on their account equity. If their equity dips below the moving average (signalling a downtrend in performance), they reduce risk or stop trading until it crosses back up.

This is a mechanical way to enforce the idea of trading smaller during drawdowns and pressing harder during winning periods. You could also set specific equity thresholds: e.g., "If I draw down 10% from the peak, I will only trade half my normal size until I've made back at least 5%."

These kinds of rules create a dynamic position sizing approach that automatically belts you in during the bumpy rides. Another technique: track your rolling 20-trade win rate or profit factor. If those metrics deteriorate beyond a certain point, that triggers a timeout or review.

Essentially, if you’re a retail trader treat your trading like a business that has performance audits. When a factory's output quality drops, they pause and service the machinery. Likewise, when your equity is sliding, something might need fixing, either in you or the strategy. Having predefined triggers removes some of the discretion (and thus emotion) from the decision to pull back.

## Journaling and Emotional Processing

I’ve talked about the importance of journaling to analyse mistakes, but it's equally important for processing your emotions during a drawdown. Write down how you're feeling throughout the slump. Whether you’re scared, angry, or hopeless? Getting it on paper (or screen) can be cleansing and often reveals the emotional triggers that might sabotage you.

For instance, journaling might reveal "After my third loss I started feeling like I had to win the next trade, and that's when things went off the rails." That awareness is huge; you can then work on countering it (maybe by taking a break/going on a walk after 3 losses to reset your mindset).

Also, it seems silly, but documenting small victories during a recovery can rebuild confidence. If you journal, "Today I stuck to my plan on three trades, even though two were losses, I'm proud I didn't revenge trade," that's progress. Reading your journal entries from past drawdowns where you did come back can also boost morale. It's proof that "this too shall pass."

A journal shows you the truth of what's going on inside you. In a deep drawdown, when your mind might play tricks (telling you you're awful, or that you must try something radical), the journal provides perspective and continuity. It reminds you of who you are at your best and what you've learned from prior mistakes. Use it not only to track numbers but also the psychological journey.

## Take a Step Back to Step Forward

Sometimes, the best move in a drawdown is to literally do less. This could mean taking a short hiatus from trading. A few days off to clear your head, get good sleep, maybe do some exercise or hobbies, can work wonders. If your budget can afford it, go on holiday. It's not running away; it's recharging your mental capital. When you return, you'll see the market with fresh eyes more often than not.

Some traders have routines for drawdown recovery: for example, going back to basics, trading the basics and keeping it boring until they start winning again. It's like a basketball player seeing the ball go through the hoop on a simple layup to break a cold streak. Get a win, even a small one, to rebuild positive momentum.

Lastly, make sure you're taking care of your health during a drawdown – it's easy to lose sleep or stress-eat junk food (or worse) when worried about losses. But that only makes you feel worse and less sharp. Treat yourself like an athlete in rehabilitation: plenty of rest with good nutrition. The goal is to emerge from the drawdown not just financially intact, but mentally stronger.

## Reconnect with the Long-Term Mission

When you're down in the equity valley, it helps to zoom out and remember why you trade and what your long-term goals are. If you're a retail trader trying to build a nest egg or achieve financial freedom, know that the path is never linear.

Even the best money managers in the world have equity curves that look like staircases – up, then a step down, then up again, and so on. A drawdown is just one step in that staircase. It's not permanent unless you quit or blow up.

By reframing a drawdown as a temporary setback on a long journey, you reduce the overwhelming feeling that "all is lost." One technique some use is to imagine telling the story of their trading career in the future: "I went through a brutal 25% drawdown that tested everything I had, but I managed to cut back, stick to my risk rules, and slowly climb out of it. That experience taught me more about myself than any winning streak ever could."

By almost telling the story of your struggle as if it's already overcome, you instil confidence that you will overcome it. It sounds a bit fluffy, but your mindset is crucial. Trading is a game of mental capital as much as financial capital.

## Building Resilience: My own lessons

Having survived multiple cycles of gains and gut-wrenching drawdowns over the years, I'll leave you with some candid perspective. Managing money is a long game. Drawdowns are not only inevitable; they're necessary. They humble you, teach you about your weaknesses, and force you to evolve. Every significant drawdown I endured ultimately made me better – but only because I survived it and took the lessons to heart. Here are a few distilled insights to carry with you:

### Always Play Defence First

Newer participants fixate on how to make money; veterans focus on how not to lose too much money. Capital preservation is the foundation. If you protect your downside rigorously (with stops, position sizing, and drawdown control), the upside tends to take care of itself over time.

I often tell folks: My number one goal is to stay in the game. When you survive, you give yourself the chance to hit new highs down the road. Blow up once, and it's over in my opinion. So take pride in your risk management as much as your profits – surviving a brutal market onslaught with only a small loss is a victory.

### Embrace the Drawdown as Feedback, Not Failure

It's easy to label a drawdown as a failure or a setback. Reframe it as feedback from the market. It's information. It could be telling you about a flaw in your strategy, or maybe pushing you to develop more emotional discipline. Ask yourself "What is this drawdown teaching me?" Maybe that you were too concentrated in one asset? Or that you need to adapt to a new regime?

A growth mindset will extract value from the experience. By viewing it this way, you also distance yourself from the drawdown. It's not you that's a failure; it's perhaps your approach that needs tweaking, which is fixable. And sometimes, the lesson is simply that you're human and had a lapse of discipline, which you can correct.

In my early career, I had a drawdown so bad that most would have quit. In hindsight, that episode taught me risk management rules I likely never would have internalised otherwise. The pain was the teacher. So, as odd as it sounds, be grateful for the lesson but make sure to learn from it and not to repeat it.

### Keep a Long-Term Perspective

When you zoom out on a multi-year equity curve of a successful money manager, it's never a straight line up. It looks more like a sawtooth that angles upward. Those teeth-like dips are drawdowns. If you're in one now, remind yourself that this is just one "tooth" in the grand scheme. The next leg up could be right around the corner – if you stay solvent and sane.

Think of trading as spanning decades, not weeks. A single drawdown is a chapter, not your whole story.

### Emotional Honesty and Self-Care

One thing I've learned is to be brutally honest with myself about how I'm feeling, especially in tough times. If you're scared, admit it. If you're revengeful, admit it. Only then can you address it. Talk to someone – another participant who understands, or even just journal it out – about your fears. It's really good for the brain.

Also, take care of yourself outside trading. In a drawdown, double down on healthy habits: exercise, sleep, time with loved ones, hobbies. It might feel like you should spend 18 hours a day glued to the screens until you make the money back, but that often just digs the hole deeper.

By maintaining a balanced lifestyle, you ensure that a drawdown in trading doesn't become a drawdown in your overall well-being. There were times I let a slump isolate me and completely consume my mind – it never helped. When I finally snapped out, often after a friend dragged me out to do something fun, played golf, or I hit the gym consistently, I came back fresher and quickly traded better. Your mind is your primary tool in markets; keep it in good shape.

### Have a Plan for Recovery

Don't wait until you're in the depths of a drawdown to figure out how to handle it. Make a drawdown plan when you're calm. Even if that’s just writing down: "If I draw down 10%, I will do X. If 20%, I will do Y." Maybe X is "cut trading size in half and no new strategies" and Y is "take one week off and thoroughly review everything."

Include in the plan some of the strategies from above: at what point will you reduce risk, at what point will you stop and step away? This way, when you're actually in the heat of it, you don't have to rely on willpower or clear thinking (which are in short supply then) – you just follow your predetermined plan. It's like having a fire drill.

### My Personal Approach to Drawdowns

Unlike many, I don't actually believe in arbitrarily reducing exposures just because I'm in a drawdown. I literally wouldn't change a position simply because it's losing money. Instead, I maintain a strict drawdown stop-out framework for my entire book. My system is straightforward: I will reduce exposure at 5% drawdown for the year, and if we reach 7-8%, the whole book gets cut. That's my maximum threshold. This is important to note - I'm measuring from January 1st each year, not from the highest point my account reached (peak to trough).

When a position is performing poorly, my first step isn't to automatically reduce it – it's to thoroughly examine my strategy, rules, and systems to determine what's actually happening. If I conclude my strategy is fundamentally flawed, only then will I change things up or cut the position entirely. Sometimes the issue isn't whether to hold or fold, but how the position is structured. Perhaps I shouldn't have bought the underlying directly and should have structured the trade via a call spread instead to limit downside.

I've found that one of the most effective ways to minimise drawdowns before they happen is to carefully analyse the strength of the trend. If a trend is showing weakness, I'll proactively keep the position size down rather than waiting for losses to accumulate. This preemptive approach to position sizing based on trend strength has saved me from numerous deeper drawdowns. I'll dive much deeper into these concepts in the upcoming risk management section of this post.

Drawdowns hurt. They test our patience, our confidence, and sometimes who we are as a person. But they are also what forge us into seasoned participants. You do not truly know your risk tolerance and psychological makeup as a trader until you've been through a wicked drawdown and clawed your way out. In the market's grand game of attrition, those who manage drawdowns intelligently and gracefully are the ones left standing to reap the long-term rewards. Remember, markets aren’t about being right every time - it's about handling the times you're wrong in a way that preserves your capital and your mind.

So the next time you find yourself staring at a screen of red or watching your hard-earned equity slip away, take a deep breath. Recognise whether you're dealing with a trade-level issue or a portfolio-level issue (do I need to adjust overall strategy or take a break?). Deploy the tactics we discussed: cut your risk, honour your stops, analyse what went wrong, and most of all, keep your head.

Every trader's journey is dotted with drawdown valleys - but it's in the valleys that you sharpen your skills and steel your resolve for the peaks to come. Stay disciplined, stay humble, and keep going. The climb out is not easy, but for those who persevere, reaching new equity highs again is not just possible, it's probable.

Keep fighting the good fight, manage those drawdowns, and you'll live to capture the next big opportunity that the markets throw your way.
