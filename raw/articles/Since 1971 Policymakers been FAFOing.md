I woke up yesterday with a pressing need to review policymaker action through history to see what they did, why they may have done it, and what the stock market did subsequent to their actions.

When I think about policymakers I am referring to Monetary Policy and Fiscal policy. Back before QE, Monetary policy was dominated by changes in the short term interest rate and the money supply. Since QE, the balance sheet was also used to impact asset prices. Fiscal policy prior to the Yellen administration, was dominated by choices by the legislature and executive branches to tax and spend. Since the QE era, and particularly since the use of the Fed’s balance sheet has been the dominant lever for monetary policy, the excecutive branch controlled monetary policy. Trump 1.0 and Mnuchin either didn’t understand this opportunity or didnt want to use the lever. But Janet Yellen knew exactly how it worked and used it aggresively to replace the Fed in terms of the balance sheet lever of monetary policy.

While the executive. branch controlling monetary policy may seem new to many, it was all the result of the single most important monetary policy decision in the post world war II era. Monetary policy was forever changed when the executive branch abandoned Bretton Woods and the gold standard. Not only was it a massive debasement and easing but it also enabled the deficit spending regime of the past 55 years.

Perhaps the most obvious trend which matter really started in 1971. Nixon changed the world by rejecting the Breton Woods agreement. That has set the trend of Fiat debasement which hasnt markedly changed in 55 years. The other great trend that was unlocked by Nixon rejecting Breton Woods which ultimately was levered by many leaders was the massive growth in US government debt. Prior to 1970 US Government debt growth was essentially flat for 2 decades. Prior to that debt growth only occured during kinetic world wars.

**Framework**

*Crisis*

Perhaps the simplest way to think about policymaker action is they are ALWAYS going to act during a crisis. Will they act promptly? Will they act adequately at first? That’s mostly tweaks. They go big in crisis is a virtual certainty.

*Overheating*

The rare thing is for policymakers to throw cold water on an economy. It happens. but is pretty rare

*Change of political control*

Another set of policy changes occur when fiscal policymakers flip from

- One party controlling both the Legislative and Executive branch to some form of gridlock in which the flip results in reigning in policy agendas
- More impactfully when control flips fully from one party to another or from gridlock to full party control.

This sort of policy change can occur during a crisis, when the economy is overheating, or somewhere in between. the First two reasons will be dealt with in the first two pillars while I will limit this pillar to policy changes during “normal times”

*Election pumping*

One other potential reason for a policy change seems to stoke the most noise amongst market participants lately. This would be policy changes that precede a Presidential or mid term election occurs done by policymakers to ensure the incumbent wins reelection. **I will look at this multi decade period for major policy changes that were not needed to address a crisis, an overheating, nor subsequent to a recent political reorientation (because that was the prior year), but, was purely politica** l

While some say the Fed has become uber political and acted to attempt to prevent Trump from being reelected in 2024 we will mostly be focusing on the prior 55 years of what appears to us to be a largely apolitical Fed. We of course are looking at Yellen very carefully and in particular the Yellen/Powell/Waller pivot on Halloween 2023 as being politically motivated but believe what happened was a policymaker overreaction to the August - Halloween 2023 asset selloff that led to the actions taken and once taken these fiscal policymakers certainly didn’t attempt to cool off the rebound but didn’t stoke it further. But I will posit the Fed may have been political with the 50bp pre-election cut. Mostly cuz I don’t know.

So the categories are

- Crisis
- Overheating
- Political control flip - neutral conditions (implemented in first year)
- Pre election pump - neutral conditions (by definition in second year of congressional terms

Another cross section of these categories are whether the two policymakers are

- Aligned and both acting
- Aligned and one acting
- Only one is acting the other is neutral
- Operating at cross purposes.

So what are my big takeaways.

- I catalog 37 major policymaker shifts. They pretty much all have major impacts on equity markets.
- Half of them were in response to a crisis and every crisis response but one resulted in significant and fairly immediate stock price rallies
- The Fed has in the past been willing to act to cool an overheating economy despite the fact that most but not all of the time the markets selloff.
- Even Fiscal occasionally attempts to cool an overheating economy and when they do it results in a sizable negative market reaction
- Its pretty rare that the policy changes are the result of political control shifts as those are rare to begin with. But they have meaningful impact
- It is almost unheard of for policy makers to shift policy for no other reason than to win an election or even more so a mid term election.
- Fiscal and monetary policymakers are almost always aligned in a crisis while only some of the time does fiscal pull on the stimulus oar leaving monetary policy to do most of the work
- The Fed pretty much is on its own in cooling the economy and the Executive branch except in one circumstance either is neutral or on rare occassions operates at cross purposes.

So the big question is what does the mean for the future. I would

- Be certain that in an economic crisis the entire set of policymakers will act to counteract the crisis
- Expect in a true and obvious overheating the Monetary policymakers will act to slow the economy. But because we have had various people leading the Fed and they have been influenced by others and also possibly unwilling to work till the job is done its important to assess who is in the seat and what will trigger a policy stance that the executive branch doesn’t like.
- Expect that when fiscal policy authorities hold both the executive office and the majority in the legislature that in the first year of a presidential cycle that policy choices will matter.
- Significantly discount any narrative that says that policymakers will take action without a crisis simply to get re-elected and particularly for mid terms.
