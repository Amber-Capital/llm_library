With much trepidation and a great deal of humility I decided to write down my thoughts about choosing what to trade. Trepidation, because this is a complex topic which is difficult to explain conceptually and even more difficult to explain in detail. Humility, because some readers, particularly anyone who may have built a multi alpha stream, understand this topic in much greater detail than I do. I have built many such alpha machines over my career and have worked at some of the best firms on the planet who have systemized such machines or successfully cobbled together discretionary diversified alpha streams. Despite my credentials and experience this topic is extremely sophisticated and as I said I have trepidation trying to make this topic simple. Hopefully those who read it can learn something and those who understand this topic better than I can accept my goal is to stay only at a conceptual simplistic level.

Preamble complete.

One of the Members of Dampedspring.com asked “. If u could pls a 101/201 on trade expectancy / payoff vs frequency: waiting on the sidelines for a fat pitch vs being in a trade w less skewed r:r. Math/theory less interesting than anecdotes from the trenches, personal musings pro/con etc, inside view.” So this is the question I will try to answer by describing the concepts at play.

**The first and highest level concept I want to start with is that it is very unlikely that one has an edge in trading markets.** As I say in pretty much every piece of work I write, your job as an investor is to buy a diversified portfolio of assets that is well balanced to all possible economic outcomes and mostly hold these assets passively for your entire lifetime. But you don’t want to hear that because this is about active trading.

If you are trading actively you are essentially gambling. Active trading is most similar to paramutual betting systems like horse racing. Assuming that the “house” doesn’t take anything for providing the paramutual betting system those who are betting on the outcome of the race have a zero sum outcome. Any money made by winners is money lost by losers. The odds/prices of the bets on the horses are established by and fluctuate with the bets made.

Like horse racing the markets are zero sum when it comes to timing markets. At this very moment someone owns every single financial asset which if held for the long term provide a positive return for the holders vs cash. But any active trader is attempting to outperform that “beta” return or “risk premium”. They choose to hold a portion of the market portfolio that either overweight/underweigh an asset relative to the market basket or choose to hold cash or hold assets with leverage. By making these choices, say to overweight stocks, and short bonds, they are forcing another active trader to underweight stocks and overweight bonds. Just as in horse racing the winners take from the losers

Oh by the way. The “house” takes all bettors money in both horse racing and financial markets trading.

So basically I only want to trade if I believe I have a positive expected risk adjusted return. Of course my counterparty is likely to only want to trade for the same reason. Over time with many bets one of us will be proven wrong and given the “house” take both of us could be proven wrong.

Okay enough discouraging talk. Lets stipulate you have edge in betting on markets. Furthermore you have edge in trading many different markets and many different strategies. Here are some concepts I think are worth considering.

- How would you rank your strategies?
- Benefits of diversification
- Are you applying a single strategy to multiple markets?
- Are you applying multiple strategies to the same market?
- What constraints prevent you from exploiting each strategy
- How much of your resources should you spend on new strategies versus improving existing strategies?

Lets give some examples. Let’s say you are a poker player and a horse racing handicapper and you are a better poker player than handicapper. Also you are considering becoming a sports bettor.

Your strategies for winning at poker are:

- Calculating the odds of winning a hand based on the information available and the sizing of the pot better than anyone you play
- Reading other players actions

Your strategies for winning at the track are

- Fundamental evaluation of horses from every aspect of their presentation, and track record which reliably allows you to beta the odds offered
- Reading other bettors actions

You hope to enter sports betting but for now you have only one edge

- Reading other bettors actions.

**Ranking strategies**

This is tricky. In the example above there are three active strategies at play. You are faster and better at odds calculation, good at assessing horse flesh, and good at reading others. Ideally I would want to know how good each strategy is even though the “track record” is blurred by a common strategy used in both active systems. Unpacking this is complicated in that its possible if not likely that each “game” requires different amounts of the common strategy. For instance lets say your highest ranked strategy is reading other bettors and at the same time poker success depends much more on reading other bettors than handicapping. In that case its possible that you are actually not that great at calculating the odds of a poker hand relative to horse flesh. You happen to be better at poker but its not because of your idiosyncratic skills in each game. Alternatively you could be mediocre in the common skill which should hurt your poker performance which depends on reading people more than horse racing but you are really awesome at the idiosyncratic part of poker relative to horsec racing. For weighting your various bets and understanding where to devote resources to improvement of existing systems and/or entering sports betting you need to understand these things.

**Benefits of diversification**

Trading the same strategy on two different markets at the same time is a tradeoff with trading the market at which you are best vs. diversification across markets. Trading multiple strategies on a single market is a tradeoff between trading your best strategy vs diversification across strategies. In both cases diversification can occur. Ill describe below

**Common Strategy different markets**

Lets say you have a momentum strategy that you apply to many markets. It is likely that the trades in your book are all correlated to a momentum factor which means you get limited diversification to momentum. However you may get diversification from macro economic variables and idiosyncratic risk of the underlying asset classes you trade. For instance one day you may be long stocks and short bonds and the next day you may be long stocks and long bonds. While the strategy is not a diversifier the the asset class is. If your strategy is equally “good” in both stocks and bonds holding equal risk in both markets is sensible because it lowers risk while not changing return.

**Different Strategies same market.**

Let’s say you have a momentum strategy and a macro economic strategy which leads the market and those two strategies are equally good and over time hold meaningfully different positions at any snapshot. Essentially the two strategies are uncorrelated and both “good”. By pursuing equal risk allocation to both strategies you reduce the risk while maintaining the return. In this case you are not benefitting from asset diversification but from strategy diversification

**Tradeoffs for diversification**

Unfortunately some markets have more opportunity and some strategies you have may be better than others. So when allocating you have to judge the amount of risk to hold in each strategy and each market. Your highest return is going to be to implement your best strategy in the best market. But there is a place for lesser strategies if either the lesser markets or strategy are diversifiers. What you want is the highest risk adjusted return of your overall edge through time. Going from one market and one strategy to two markets or two strategies is a big and valuable step. However adding a new strategy or market when you are already trading 10 uncorrelated strategies applied across 10 uncorrelated markets is much much less valuable.

**Constraints**

Depending on who you are you may not be able to participate in certain markets or implement strategies adequately cheaply. You may have inadequate capital to trade the strategy. If you are large you may want to implement your strategy in a market too small to accomodate a reasonable allocation. But most importantly choosing to seek diversification with multiple strategies across multiple markets is complicated for even the most sophiscated hedge fund. Constraints auger for simplicity at the expense of diversification.

**Resources**

Every person and every organization is resource constrained. Those resources are both capital and time. Allocating both requires complicated tradeoffs. One must maintain and improving existing edge as a baseline. If there is extra resources new markets may be even better than existing markets and diversifying. But at a point diversifying benefit ends. Capital growth at successful funds for finding new strategies and markets to trade. Many great firms have been pushed to expand as the capital they had could not be allocated to existing strategies effectively. Its often called style drift. Style drift is not in itself a bad thing. If one knows their source of alpha and applies it effectively to other asset classes in other markets while keeping laser focused on whether they are actually diversifying a firm can grow and prosper but most end up making a bad tradeoff between “goodness”, false diversification, and the press of AUM growth.

**Synthesis**

In order to choose what to trade it is critical to know what good is in some detail. It is critical to not allocate capital to anything at which you are not adequately good. This is a crazy competitive game and staying in your lane is essential. Otoh knowing what your competitive advantage is allows one to add markets and strategies that are beneficial to your long term results. If you have a single strategy in a single market it is pretty likely that if that works in one market it will work in others and the diversification benefit could be sizeable. If you have a single strategy and time and resoources to build another strategy which is good but not as good and different than your original strategy the diversification benefit of building that strategy can be well worth doing. All of these choices are tradeoffs. One last point. Every strategy dies eventually. No matter how many you have deployed across many markets devoting some portion of your limited resources to exploration is something worth doing regardless of who you are imho.
