Over the last few weeks, and likely much more often than we have been used to over the past 17 years, money markets have had some temporary volatility. Repo rates briefly moved above the Fed’s target range for short term interest rates indicating demand for cash. In the banking system the Fed’s standing repo facility briefly moved from zero usage to a peak of 50BN usage. Ironically while the standing repo facility was “spiking” so was the reverse repo program in equal measure. This is ironic because on literally the same day some bank or a few banks needed 50BN in cash while at the same moment other banks, or MMF’s had 50BN of cash looking for a home. These two ships passed in the night and the Fed took out a spread paying the RRP rate to the lenders and charging the higher SRF rate to the borrowers.

**What is happening?**

Firstly, this money market volatility is absolutely normal in any pre GFC context. Post GFC there was essentially no money market volatility. Only in 2019 was there significant volatility. Compared to NO volatility some volatility looks like a lot. It is not. Secondly, the Fed has successfully reduced excess and abundant liquidity in the banking system by reducing its balance sheet by over $2TN and draining the pure excess liquidity of the RRP to zero and draining reserves modestly. Thirdly, some banks will have some risk of having adequate reserves to settle the transactions they and their clients pursue each day now that reserves are less excessive which will result in ongoing modest in context of history money market volatility. BUT, Lastly, the banking system as a whole is in awesome shape from a capital standpoint and a credit portfolio standpoint and the Fed has programs already stood up which will mitigate any meaningful stress.

**Why do banks need reserves to begin with?**

Many years ago bank reserves served two purposes. Today they serve only one direct purpose. Today the reason why banks hold reserves is for transaction settlement. Those transactions that require reserves are

- Every transaction done by a bank or a banking client that has another bank or another bank’s client as a counterparty
- Every transaction done by a bank or its clients with the Federal Reserve.

Every transaction of a good, service, asset, dividends, or interest, etc, is between two different parties. The payer and receiver of payment. If the payer and receiver are different banks a bank deposit moves from one bank to another and that liability transfer is accompanied with an asset transfer. That asset that is transferred between banks or between a bank and the Fed is a bank reserve. Bank reserves are literally the grease that allows the economy and its banks to transact smoothly.

In years past reserves also served a second purpose. Regulators required a minimum reserve to asset ratio. If a bank hit is minimum required reserves it could no longer create credit. This was called a fractional reserve banking system. Over the past decades the US banking system has moved toward a risk based regulatory framework. So has the rest of the global banking system and the Basel Framework is an attempt to have one global standard. In the US since 2020 the minimum reserves requirement has been abandoned. This article is not about the new framework is about the purpose for reserves and as of today the purpose is simply the grease that allows daily transactions to settle.

**How can banks run low on reserves**

A bank has limited control on choices made by its client base. Banks also have obligations that come due based on the calendar which once set can be prepared for but require planning. Banks also have their own business model which may want to seize on a particular opportuity to transact for their own account. As mentioned above every one of the resulting transactions if with either another bank, another bank’s client, or the Fed will require a reserve transfer. So reserves change at an individual bank level as they and their clients transact. Sometime they rise as they and their clients sell stuff and sometimes they fall as they and their clients buy stuff.

**Why do different banks keep different reserve balances**

Banks get to choose their level of reserves. If they want more they can sell assets to other banks or the Fed, or they can pay up higher interest on deposits and other liabilities to get reserves from banks who won’t compete and lose their deposits and reserves, or they can even sell equity. In normal times with normal liquidity conditions a solvent bank has a ton of options to manage their reserves. BUT it has a cost. A reserve pays IORB interest which is pretty much the lowest risk free form of interest a bank can invest its assets in. More reserves mean more crappy interest and less interest from higher interest assets, more interest paid to depositors or equity dilution. Because there is a cost a bank tries to maximize its return to shareholders by minimizing its reserves balances. However running reserves too low creates a risk. That risk is mostly short term financial risk but can lead to more catastrophic risk. In terms of short term if a bank runs its reserves below their comfort zone they have to act. The action will have a penalty. That penalty may be short term but sharp enough to be expensive or it may be longer term and result in forced action which can be very expensive. Let’s assume most banks are better than we are at doing this. It’s fun to be cynical and think bankers are frauds and ignorant and the regulators are always asleep at the switch. But I operate with the assumption that these folks know how to do their job and try hard to do it well.

So lets assume a bank is trying to balance the cost of running with excess reserves vs the risk of running with scarce reserves. What determines the right level. Well its different depending on the specific banks book of business. Every bank has a different client base with different needs and every bank operates differetnly regarding its own propietary asset portfolio of securities. While many of these issues are common across the banking system each bank attempts to

- Determine the size and frequency of its clients transaction volumes
- Model The portion of the above transaction volumes that are with another bank or another bank’s clients
- Model The activity of their client base in buying primary auction issuance which results in a reserve outflow to the Fed and maturities of their clients treasury holdings which results in a reserve inflow
- Model their proprietary transactions
- Look at historical actual reserve volatility to validate models
- Stress test to make sure the odds of a catastrohic reserve outflow are low.

The Regulators also model this and use the banks models to understand the banking volumes and reserve flows.

So thats pretty much the way it works. Excess reserves have a cost, too low reserves have risk, and just right depends on the bank and its book of business.

**What short term actions are available to banks when they run low on reserves?**

As mentioned above its likely as some banks have low reserves and overall reserves have fallen from abundant to ample some banks both big and small will do a set of daily transactions that will require large scale reserve transfer. They may not have them. Importantly every major asset market besides the money markets settle transactions t+1. That means there are limited short term options for a bank with a need for reserveses. They have three major choices

- Borrow reserves from another bank (this used to be very common but less so now)
- Enter into a repo transaction where they get cash (reserves) and pledge collateral
- Borrow from the Fed via the discount window or standing repo facility which has a higher cost typically as well as perhaps a negative stigma but offers a wider range of collateral options for banks without lots of unencumberedf bills and treasuries to pledge

This is what happened last month. A bank or two got squeezed by tga bills issuance and needed a few days of repo and SFR to get past it such that they could rebalance. The rebalance probably didnt even require any action at all but just was ebbs and flows of their book of business.

**What other actions are available to banks when they persistently run low on reserves**

So lets say a few banks decide they have been running a bit to low on reserve balances and want to add reserves. They have tons of options to raise reserves. In order of market impact and quickness to achieve the goal the options are

- Sell highly liquid assets like tbills, treasuries and MBS
- Sell less liquid assets like loans
- Sell corporate debt.
- Sell equity, preferred, or convertibles
- Offer and sell CD’s
- Compete for interest bearing savings

**How does all of the above lead to what is likely to be some more volatility in money markets.**

I like to think about the reserves topic with the Warren Buffett metaphor. The tide has been running out due to QT. There are few guys that are swimming naked but everyone is still “hidden”. Every so often you are going to see someones junk. BUT the tide has stopped running out. For now the tide is low enough that its going to get more messy than it had been. Perhaps everyone keeps swimming and doesnt rebalance in the long term ways I describe. Perhaps the regulators force some of the naked guys to act (though they have no regulatory teeth they may have coercive tactics that force action - That’s another story from my past when a regulator wanted something from me and I resisted breifly lol) But perhaps the whole thing will just be slightly more volatile and thats what it will be. That would likely be fine. IF however the tide is simply way too low and stresses exist and persist for weeks and months and for example the SRF stays at 100BN every day the Fed will release the tide of Reserve management.

Hopefully you get how all that plays together and dont panic when the doomers say the financing market is blowing up and YOU do act when a systemwide credit event does occur. Knowing the difference is key
