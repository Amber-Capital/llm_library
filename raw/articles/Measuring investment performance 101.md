I have some strong opinions on this topic. It is also one of the most studied topics in finance. I'll give it a go.

I'll start with my strongest view. It is almost impossible to determine if an investor has an ability to beat the market

---

Beating the market is extremely difficult and though a very few may be able to do it almost all investors are unable to do it

Track records are useless as predictors of future returns in most examples

Of 1000 portfolio managers timing markets 172 managers will beat the market

---

7 or more times out of 10 years by pure luck. That means one has to have a very high bar for anyone claiming to have beat the market because it's probably luck

All track records are too short to be statistically significant.

---

Well jeez. Given all that what should someone do. The first answer is easy. Don't try. Invest in a widely and truly diversified set of investments that are passive and pay no or low transaction costs and charge you no or low fees. John Bogle school.

If you must try

---

Here is what I want to see. 1. The longest possible track record.  
2\. A systematic process that has no or low discretionary input that can be back tested for a longer period of time than the actual track record  
3\. The proper benchmark that is highly correlated to the portolio

---

In question and is at the same level of risk and leverage.

With that and the assumption that luck is the most likely cause of outperformance and alpha is really hard and most don't have it consider the risk adjusted return in a broad way. Things like Sharpe ratio, drawdown

---

From peak, sortino ratio and all the other versions of track record evaluation may provide some insight but again are almost certainly inadequate and statistically insignificant.

So it's almost certain that you will fail to generate any certainty that a PM can beat the market

---

With track record. What other factors may help

By far my most important question I ask a PM is who are you talking your alpha from. Who is losing money that you make and why. The answers have many red flags. For instance if the words used by the candidate suggest the other

---

side is dumb, or retail, runaway as fast as you can. That sort of arrogance reveals ignorance.

Try to identify if the candidate has a information advantage that is reliable and legal

identify if the pm is providing a service to markets and receiving fair compensation

---

Many PM's are providing liquidity for other movers of risk and these PM's receive transaction costs instead of paying transaction costs. They are not outperforming markets. The excess return is compensation for market making services. It may make it a perfectly good investment

---

But it's a different discussion

Strip out all correlation to benchmarks. Alpha is active management the outperformance should be uncorrelated to the benchmark. If it is correlated the outperformance is likely levered beta. In particular identify what markets were doing

---

When the pm experienced drawdowns. If the drawdowns are correlated strongly to the benchmark drawdowns. Run away. Again it's likely leveraged beta. Not outperformance

---

What if you are trying to evaluate your own performance. Mostly focus on your investment process. Do you have one. How does it work. If you violate it what is the track record. The same

---

Problems of track record and luck apply to you. The problem you may have is lying to yourself. This is the trickiest part. My strong suggestion is to ask someone to interview you and ask hard questions like the ones above. You will find it extremely humbling
