**The boring part**

After describing all our concerns about major shifts that may need to be addressed to create a more resilient long only portfolio, it is now time for the boring part. Why invest in a long-only portfolio of assets in the first place? Let’s start with savings. There is no God-given mandate for people to save. We could consume every penny we earn. Many people are unable to save at all. Some people subsist on their earnings and government subsidies. Some borrow to spend and create negative savings. Cultures differ in terms of their savings. Also, international trade creates forced savings in cultures with trade surpluses and less saving in countries with trade deficits. Government budget deficits create forced savings in the private sector. All these things happen all at once. Nonetheless the desire to save for those who are able is clear. People save to have money:

> · For a rainy day, in case of a job loss or unexpected expense.
> 
> · For a known near-term large expense or debt repayment, like a down payment on a house or college educations for their children.
> 
> · For future consumption when they retire and no longer have income besides investment income.
> 
> · To pass on to charities or heirs upon their death.

So, given that people save money, the question is: what is the best way to save? The simplest and least risky way to save is with Money Market Funds, Bank Deposits, or T-Bills. From now on, we will call that cash. Cash pays interest, in part to compensate cash savers for the daily hit to purchasing power that inflation causes. On that note, we are going to stay out of the weeds regarding whether that compensation is adequate or not by comparing investment portfolio returns in excess of cash returns.

The question becomes: why invest in long-term assets instead of cash? The reason to invest in assets is because they are risky and compensate you for that risk by providing excess returns relative to cash. An expected excess return over cash is called a risk premium. **A long-only portfolio offers free money because it collects risk premiums over time.**

***Risk premium 101***

What is risk premium? It is the extra return offered to someone with cash to buy an asset with market risk. Cash can be spent today. Cash can be used to pay off debt today or saved today in a bank or MMF or under a mattress. An asset that has market risk is not cash. Unlike cash, an asset bought today can change in value tomorrow. The reason assets require a higher expected return is because of this feature/bug. Everyone who wants cash has the same group of people (savers and banks) from whom to get cash. Those who want cash compete for the cash by offering excess returns over cash to induce someone to buy their asset. Cash represents a floor return for all investors because it is a widely available zero risk alternative. No risk premium offered? No cash available to you.

How can all assets have a risk premium when some assets go up and some go down each day, month, and year? This is a good question. It's about expectations. At this very moment, all assets have a risk premium. Someone currently owns every asset, and they think it will go up. Unfortunately, we cannot know which assets will go up. If we did, everyone would move to those assets and push up the price. However, as of this very moment, all future expectations for assets are priced in for this discussion. Sure, some assets will go up and some will go down based on changes in fundamental economic outcomes and expectations of changes. But what is important is that, despite the moves, things that go down go down less and things that go up go up more because they collect risk premium.

How much risk premium does each asset get? Two factors dominate the risk premium of each asset:

> · How risky is the asset?
> 
> · What is the idiosyncratic and market-wide supply of assets and demand from savers?

*Risk*

The less market risk, the less risk premium. A one-year Treasury will have little risk premium, while a meme stock will have a lot. The simple concept is that all assets should have the same risk-adjusted return. In other words, a higher risk asset should have a higher expected return than a lower risk asset. The expected return should scale linearly with the risk. If this were not true and one could generate higher risk-adjusted returns in a specific asset, money would flow into the asset and arbitrage away the excess. It is important to realize that all assets have a return that is greater than the return on cash because assets carry market risk and no one would buy them with cash if they had a negative expected return versus cash.

(We will briefly sidetrack this section to say there are some assets that are so important to investors for diversification purposes and in such short supply that it is possible if not certain that assets exist with a negative risk premium. Gold has historically offered negative excess returns versus cash for this reason.)

We have said that all assets have a risk premium and that risk premium scales with market risk, such that the expected risk adjusted return of all assets is the same. Before we address what influences the level of risk-adjusted returns, we will mention what we mean by risk. What we mean by risk is amorphous and pinning it down precisely will not really help much. It is some combination of future expected volatility and future drawdown risk. But we do not really know, so it's just a concept.

*Supply and Demand*

We have said that all risk-adjusted returns are the same. But what drives the level of risk-adjusted return that investors can achieve, and are issuers willing to pay, for cash? In today's world, the supply of risk assets and the demand from investors has been especially important. The level of market wide risk premium is driven by the availability of cash (money and credit) versus the supply of assets (which includes real and financial assets). When cash is plentiful and issuers of debt and equity need little cash, risk premiums tend to be low. When cash is tight and/or issuers are looking to sell a lot of debt and equity, risk premiums tend to be high.

(For the equity guys: Risk premium is inverse to multiples. Higher risk premiums equal lower multiples. For the credit gals: Risk premium is directly related to credit spreads. Rates guys: Risk premium is imbedded in both real rate products and nominal rate product yields.)

When we track risk premiums, we focus on expected asset risk, asset portfolios, and the availability of money and credit to hold and buy assets versus the risk-adjusted supply of assets looking for cash.

***Why diversify?***

Harry Markowitz described Modern Portfolio Theory in 1952. We cannot say it better than he did and recommend reading [this article](https://www.jstor.org/stable/2975974). But we will give a quick summary.

Diversification serves several important purposes in investing, based on both mathematical principles and practical risk management:

Risk Reduction Without Sacrificing Returns - The core benefit comes from combining investments that do not move in perfect lockstep. When one asset falls, others may hold steady or rise, smoothing out your overall portfolio performance. This is not just about avoiding losses - it is about achieving more predictable returns over time.

The Mathematics of Correlation - Different assets respond differently to economic events. Stocks might fall during a recession while bonds rise as investors seek safety. TIPS or gold might perform well during inflation while nominal bonds struggle. By holding assets with low or negative correlations, you can reduce portfolio volatility without necessarily reducing expected returns.

Protection Against Unforeseen Events - No one can predict which sectors, regions, or asset classes will outperform in any given period. Technology stocks dominated the 2010s, but energy led in 2022. International markets lagged the US for over a decade, then outperformed in 2017 and 2022. Diversification ensures you participate in winners while limiting damage from losers.

Time Horizon Benefits - Diversification becomes more powerful over longer periods. While a concentrated portfolio might outperform in any single year, a diversified approach typically provides more consistent compound growth over decades. This is especially valuable for retirement savings where you cannot afford major setbacks late in your career.

Behavioral Advantages - Diversified portfolios experience smaller drawdowns, making it easier to stay invested during market stress. Investors with concentrated positions often panic and sell at the worst times. Smoother performance helps maintain discipline.

Limits and Considerations - Diversification is not free. By diversifying you reduce risk and drawdown but if you diversify with assets that have lower risk (like adding bonds to an equity-only portfolio), you reduce risk but may reduce overall expected portfolio returns. However, this cost can be offset by leveraging up the less risky portfolio to the original target risk. With the use of prudent leverage, one can generate a higher absolute return with less risk. Leverage has a bad reputation, but we are not referring to the sort of speculative leverage that gets degenerate investors in trouble.

The goal is not to maximize returns in any single period, but to improve the risk-adjusted returns over your investment timeline while maintaining the emotional ability to stay the course and maintain your risk target to generate the highest possible long-term absolute returns.

***Why target a risk level?***

The question should be, Why not? Currently, most money managers assess their clients’ risk preferences via a questionnaire. The idea is to establish a risk target. Once determined, investors’ expectations can be better managed about expected returns, ex ante portfolio volatility, and drawdown risk, so when things go badly investors hopefully can avoid emotionally exiting their long-term plan. For the person managing the money (whether a DIY personal investor or a professional portfolio manager) a risk target helps a manager size positions, seek real diversification, properly leverage (or hold cash), and be aware of potential drawdown risk. **We will not spend much time on the concept but strongly recommend that every investor understand themselves and how they may react in adverse portfolio situations.**

Here is our view on setting a risk target. There is a level of change in wealth that

1\. Is absolute noise.

2\. Is slightly annoying.

3\. Pisses one off but does not cause upset or any real pain or stress.

4\. Causes a loss of sleep.

5\. Causes an action to ease the pain.

6\. Causes a life changing loss.

7\. Causes a loss so horrible that one cannot ever recover one’s current lifestyle.

These levels are personal to everyone. But we assure you everyone has levels like this. We have learned that 5-7 are not something we are willing to experience. We have experienced a level 5 event often and close to a level 6 and do not want to have that experience again. So, we operate somewhere in the range of 3-4. This range places us at a risk target we can manage, and we accept the expected return that is consistent with that level of risk. We do not want to be all-in. Staying in the game is much more important.

If you have never experienced a level 4 or 5, it is fair to say you are not taking enough risk but ideally you do not have to experience them and can just imagine them. The risk of experiencing a level 5 event is that it could turn into a level 6 event (which could be a life changing loss) or a level 7 event (which would be a loss so horrible you could not recover to anything like your current lifestyle).

All those risk levels are out there for you to experience, and we encourage you to really understand that and hold a portfolio that takes risk but does not allow for 6-7 at all. Even an unlevered portfolio is shockingly levered when you consider your future liabilities of paying for your lifestyle and your family’s education etc.

***Leverage versus risk***

Leverage is a bad word. If you say that you are going to borrow money to invest, people will be concerned. These same people will also buy a house with a reasonable mortgage and think nothing of it. They will own concentrated stock portfolios with 40% annual price volatility when you are talking about leveraging a long index fund position at 1.5x.

The important part of portfolio management is having a portfolio with the risk that you wish and the holdings you want to own. The way we start is to build a portfolio of assets we want. Then we back test the range of possible future prices for that portfolio. As mentioned above, the measures that really matter are volatility and drawdown, although there are others. Volatility is the up and down swings over a measurement period. We use a few measurement periods, including daily, weekly, monthly, and annually. Drawdown is the size of a fall in a portfolio from any point to a lower point. It is important because when investors are in drawdown, emotions can affect judgement. We want to plan to avoid drawdowns that would force us to sell to ease the pain. This aspect of risk is important when we discuss leverage because of something called a margin call.

Ok, so we have the portfolio we want, but it is too volatile for us. It either swings too rapidly or has historic drawdowns we do not like. The upside of a more volatile portfolio is that its "expected" return is higher. But if it is too risky, it must be deleveraged. How does one deleverage? Simple: sell a slice of the portfolio until the risk is to your liking. Remember to sell each thing you have proportional to its size in your portfolio. Done! Now you have the portfolio that maximizes your return, expresses your investment view and you can live with the risk.

We started with needing to deleverage (keep some cash holdings) in a portfolio to meet your risk objectives to show that it is the risk objectives that matter most. But there are many portfolios that one can hold that do not have enough risk even if every penny of cash is spent. For us, DS Beta looks like that sort of portfolio. It is pretty low risk and, at best, barely meets our risk target. But for you it may be something completely different. The way to maximize return, express your view, and still maintain your risk level is through leverage. Put simply, that is buying more assets than you have cash to pay for. Oh boy. Now we must be cautious.

Many forms of leverage in the financial markets can result in margin calls that disrupt one's portfolio and require risk trimming, which is not part of the investment strategy. However, before we jump into security selection as a method of handling margin calls, let us step back up. If you have designed your portfolio to meet your risk goals, you should have structured the portfolio so that the possibility of a margin call is extremely remote.

Let us use an example. In the US, one can easily open a margin account. A $100,000 account can control (for example only; check your broker’s rules), $150,000 of ETFs. However, if you bought $150,000 of ETFs and the portfolio value dropped a penny, you would now have to deposit whatever loss you took or face a forced liquidation or margin call. If you keep cash in some other account to meet margin calls, that amount already must be included in your calculation of risk and leverage and expected return, so you are deleveraged. If your risk limit portfolio was not $150,000 but instead was $120,000, the chance of a margin call drops radically. If you lose 50% on your portfolio, it is now worth $60,000 and your equity is $40,000, with the result that you remain in compliance in your margin account and avoid a margin call. Obviously that loss is 1.2x what you would have lost if unlevered, but you were prepared ex ante. The point is that one can leverage a portfolio and be confident that one will not be forced out of a position via a margin call.

Let us now jump into security selection and structure. There are a lot of tradeoffs when choosing how to lever a portfolio. They include:

> · Considering an alternative asset.
> 
> · Transaction costs and fees.
> 
> · Borrowing costs.
> 
> · Unwanted exposures.
> 
> · Your experience using various products.

*Considering an alternative asset*

The easiest way to add risk is to buy something closely related to what you want to buy that is riskier than what you want. The best example is buying a 30Y bond when you might otherwise want a 10Y note. The two Treasuries are not the same but are highly related. Another example is buying a single semiconductor stock instead of the semi index. The former is riskier than the index but expresses the view.

*Transaction Costs and Fees*

There are many prepackaged leveraged investments. We strongly recommend avoiding leveraged ETFs for long-term investing. They trade off badly on all our criteria. Transaction cost and fees are also high.

*Borrowing costs*

This is a big one. Know what you pay for your leverage. For a one year holding, the benchmark "fair" borrowing cost is 1-year SOFR. That rate is currently about 3.3%. If you leverage your portfolio with any margin loan, future, or option, you should expect to pay 3.3% over the next year. However, that is before retail markup. Margin loans for retail accounts are notoriously high. They quote the rate on an overnight basis so the benchmark for comparison is about 4.33%. We have seen margin costs of 10% or higher, a level that means one cannot leverage via margin loans.

Know your margin cost. If you are paying a sizeable retail markup, consider using futures and options which have an embed leverage cost closer to institutional levels. The reason is that futures and options "borrowing costs" are arbitraged by those who can borrow and lend at fair value. This is a benefit to you.

*Unwanted exposure*

Many of you can trade options but not futures. While futures provide only desired exposure, options can be used for leverage but have some undesired exposures. The straightforward way to get prudent leverage is to use deep in the money call options. The price of that call is going depend on two things:

· 1-year interest rate (your cost of leverage).

· Extreme tail risk.

Because the option is deep in the money, the tail risk may be low but will always be something you are paying for that you do not want. What you will be left with is a 2x leveraged position in the asset you want with institutional margin cost.

*Product knowledge*

We strongly recommend not using products you do not understand. If you want leverage and have high retail margin costs, you may be tempted to try options or futures. Take it slow. Understand your own situation and make the tradeoffs within your scope and risk parameters.

***Balance***

Every asset class has its own response to its own idiosyncratic market factors, overall market factors, and sensitivity to macroeconomic outcomes. We will outline some of the thinking and the practical realities for investors in the history section below, but the concepts are worth highlighting here.

Let us continue with equities and bonds to keep it simple and focus on a single macroeconomic driver. Equities outperform bonds when growth exceeds expectations and underperform when growth does not meet expectations. If you want to argue with that statement, feel free to pick any driver that will cause one asset to outperform the other.

We have described risk targets and how leveraging a low-risk asset can achieve the same level of risk and expected return as holding an unleveraged position in a high-risk asset. The SPX is about 3x more volatile than a US Agg US Treasury Mutual fund. It also has 3x the expected return. Those facts imply that a 60/40 portfolio is long 60% of total in a 3x more volatile asset. In other words, not only are you unbalanced toward equities by the 60 versus the 40, but you have much more risk in equities. This is not very balanced. To get to risk parity, in which each asset has the same risk contribution, the notional ratio of equities to AGG is roughly 25/75.

DS Beta uses the principles of risk parity, but when those principles are applied the way we describe above, it does not necessarily result in a portfolio that is balanced to macroeconomic factors.

Let us assume bonds are very sensitive to changes in growth and that equities are less sensitive. The 60/40 portfolio has much more of its risk allocated to equities and, despite the lower sensitivity to growth, the 60/40 portfolio is likely pro-growth. On the other hand, the risk parity portfolio of 25/75 is almost certainly anti-growth.

DS Beta starts with a risk parity framework and then solves toward a balance of anti-growth/pro-growth exposures and anti-inflation/pro-inflation exposures. The inventor of this concept was Bridgewater Associates, and we are not even close to matching the sophistication or quality of the All Weather Fund, which is now offered in an ETF. Frankly, it is better than DS Beta by a lot. Nonetheless, we use DS Beta as our form of beta because it is simple and gets the 80% right while not worrying about the last 20% to perfection. There is no perfection.

***History***

The understanding of portfolio construction has evolved over the past 75 years and is still evolving. A timeline of notable accomplishments is below:

1952

Harry Markowitz publishes the paper on which all further work relies, Modern Portfolio Theory, which introduces diversification, risk targets, and the efficient frontier.

1970-1981

Harry Browne focused on adding inflation assets as a diversifier to the traditional 60/40 portfolio. In 1981, he formalized these ideas into the “Permanent Portfolio.”

1990’s – 2000’s

Bridgewater Associates and AQR create their own versions of portfolios designed to improve upon both the 60/40 and the Permanent Portfolio. Bridgewater pioneered All Weather and AQR Risk Parity. These funds changed the game by offering institutional clients a better smarter beta.

2021- Today  
The popping of the bond bubble has caused significant underperformance in bond-heavy All Weather and Risk Parity Portfolios, raising legitimate questions that we focused on above about the place of nominal bonds in investor portfolios over the next decade.

***The Global Market Portfolio***

Back when Harry Markowitz created Modern Portfolio Theory, the Global Market Portfolio was quite different than it is today. Back then, government debt (besides war debt) was a small proportion of the market portfolio. It is not surprising that the 60/40 Portfolio has been a durable concept for investors for decades. It somewhat matched the available assets in the Global Market Portfolio.
