**Another gimmick**

Over the weekend Pulte was ideating with some nonsense. His goal is to address the housing market which is frozen and in particular appeal to our populism driven political environment. It is unquestionably difficult for people in our country to afford housing. Home prices are high. Mortgage interest rates are higher than they have been earlier in this century but tbh are not particularly “high” relative to economic reality of above target inflation and strong real gdp.

Mortgage rates are down 180bp from peak just slight over two years ago.

Interest rates are not particularly high in the mortgage market. What is high and remains high is home prices. Since Covid Median home prices have gone up over 50%. Or 10% per year While in the prior two decades home prices only rose by 5% per year.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jq57!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2a578569-7a61-45f1-997d-451a607a9494_1278x616.jpeg)

Home prices are impacted by supply and demand and the value of money. Supply has been stagnant as building has been slow relative to demand and those with low mortgages have been hesistant to sell. Also inflationary monetary and fiscal policy during and post COVID have bid up all assets including homes. Lastly WFH has changed some of the social dynamics favoring housing prices over office space.

Dealing with the price of homes is not something policymakers are willing to do as policies directed at tightening monetary and fiscal expansion to reduce home prices have consequences for asset prices in general and the economy as a whole. One likely successful policy is immigration restriction which reduces demand for housing.

When thinking about housing there are many moving parts.

Perhaps the most important part is the rent vs buy decision. Within that decision some important parts are

- the cost of financing. What is the fair cost of borrowing money (or for that matter selling financial assets and missing out on return)
- The potential for appreciation of homes
- The cost of home ownership including taxes, insurance, necessary maintenance, and other costs
- Rental properties may also not offer precisely the same living experience of the home you buy and custom to your taste (though that customization can be a cost that is not recouped upon sale)
- Some simply do not have the down payment and are forced to rent
- Lastly all choices have transaction costs and frictions

It's complicated.

one thing that isn't quite as complicated is financing. The number one concept in choosing mortgages is that as a retail mortgage user you have a few choices and you can not diversify your choice. Basically you have one big mortgage in your life and it’s a big part of your asset liability mix. Your choice can have meaningful consequences to your life.

The mortgage industry is also a bunch of scumbags who are almost certainly smarter in how they can pick you off and sell you a bad deal and dumber in almost every other way in a financial advisory sense and possibly a raw IQ sense.

The mortgage industry is incentivized to closing deals. They are NOT working in your interest at all. Plus they charge a lot for their services which is buried in the interest rate but also in the points

You are fucked. They are literally out to fuck you for money. How do they do that. They have various products that tease you to take on risk. The risk they leave you with is also something you have no real chance in understanding unless you are a highly sophisticated investor (you are not). But how bad can it be? Tbh every mortgage option you could chose amongst, including a pure floating rate mortgage like most of the world uses, an adjustable rate mortgage which teases you with a low rate today for a higher rate in the future, or the standard 10,15, 30 or as mentioned by Pulte 50 year fixed mortgage, are all worth roughly the same today. Meaning you are borrowing $100 with some sort of embedded cost of 3 to 5 dollars. That means each mortgage is roughly the same level of bad deal. The only difference is the risk that each mortgage has. If you knew the future and could predict the path of interest rates, the change in home prices, and your future housing needs perfectly one of these various mortgages would be “the best” for you but you can’t. So your job is to make sure you don’t take risks you are unwilling or unable to experience once setting your mortgage. Mostly that is solved by the 30 year fixed with no penalty for early payoff. But again you pay for that flexibility.

Anyway as it relates to Pulte’s nonsense

a 50 year mortgage is not markedly different than any other mortgage in terms of the getting ripped off part. Though as institutions and banks that own your mortgage don't really need or want a 50 year they may charge you another 1% in NPV until they are confident that the market is deep. You'll see a bunch of folks taking about irrelevant issues like paying more interest for longer or being better off if you think inflation is going to be high but that’s all versions of the same fucking mortgage brokers use to get you into a deal. Particularly if the deal is the highest fee to the broker.

You have no chance whatsoever to understand the risk and reward of choosing a 30 year fixed vs a 50 year fixed and in most outcomes they will be essentially identical Use the standard deep product that everyone uses Because the fees are the cheapest. The if you want to take a market view on the path of inflation or rates feel free to do it with your savings (you shouldn’t do that either)

One other musing from Pulte this weekend was allowing an existing homeowner to let his home buyer “assume” his mortgage Now this has been floated a long time and sounds great BUT those who own mortgages would be screwed if that became law

The Government would essentially be redrafting the mortgage contract in favor of the borrower at the cost of the lender We call that price fixing and choosing winners and losers The government could try that of course but the implications would be wide ranging. Indeed the biggest beneficiary eoukdn’t even be the new home buyer it would be the existing home seller. Home prices would spike on this “solution”

In the end the 50 year is just another gimmick and housing market price fixing is highly consequential.

What is needed to increase home ownership is monetary and fiscal policy which is pro real growth, results in stable prices of asset, and goods and services prices, resulting in lower long term interest rates, encouraging construction, and growing real wages, and perhaps policy which transfers wealth from the rich homeowner to the young renter. No gimmicks or quick fixes like price fixing will do the job
